How do we know if our board is spending the right amount of time on student outcomes?

The most reliable measure is a simple time-use analysis of your meeting agendas. For at least six months, track what percentage of each meeting was spent on topics that directly relate to monitoring whether students are learning — not budgets, not facilities, not personnel, but actual student outcome data.

Our board has 47 goals. Should we cut that down? How?

Yes, immediately. A board with 47 goals has no priorities — it has a wish list. Effective governance requires a small number of specific, measurable student outcome goals with 3–5 year timeframes. A useful target: 3–7 goals, each tied directly to what students will know or be able to do.

My board keeps getting pulled into management decisions. How do I redirect without creating conflict?

This is one of the most common governance challenges, and it rarely gets better on its own. The pattern — board members taking management actions — usually has two root causes: the board hasn't defined what governance actually means for them, and the superintendent hasn't clearly claimed the management domain.
