Our board has 47 goals. Should we cut that down? How?

Yes, immediately. A board with 47 goals has no priorities — it has a wish list. Effective governance requires a small number of specific, measurable student outcome goals with 3–5 year timeframes.

What's the difference between governing and managing — and why does it matter?

Governing is setting direction and holding the organization accountable for results. Managing is deciding how to get there day-to-day. When boards blur this line — hiring custodians, approving field trips, questioning specific staff decisions — they undermine the superintendent's authority and distract themselves from the only question that matters: are students learning?

Our superintendent pushes back when we ask detailed operational questions. Are they wrong to do that?

Usually, no. A superintendent who pushes back on operational micromanagement is doing their job. The right question to ask yourself is whether your question is about student outcomes or about operations. "Are reading scores improving in grade 3?" is a governance question. "Which reading curriculum did you select?" is a management question.

Community members demand the board take action on a specific staff situation. How do we respond?

Acknowledge the concern, then redirect: the board sets policy and monitors outcomes — individual personnel decisions belong to the superintendent. If a community concern reflects a systemic outcome problem, that's a legitimate governance conversation. If it's about one employee, it isn't. Conflating the two is how boards get sued and superintendents quit.

Focus Mindset

What does a healthy board-superintendent relationship actually look like?

Clear domains. The board owns the "what" — student outcome goals, community values, policy guardrails. The superintendent owns the "how" — staffing, programs, operations. Both parties respect the line. The board evaluates the superintendent on results, not relationships. The superintendent brings the board real data, not polished presentations designed to avoid hard conversations.

Focus Mindset

I'm a newly elected board member. How do I stay focused on governance when everything feels urgent?

Write down your three most important student outcome questions and put them on the desk in front of you at every meeting. Before speaking, ask yourself whether your comment advances the answer to one of those questions. If it doesn't, stay quiet — at least for now. You'll be surprised how much of what feels urgent is actually noise.

Clarify Priorities

What makes a student outcome goal actually good?

A good student outcome goal has four qualities: it describes what students will know or be able to do (not what adults will do), it's measurable with existing or obtainable data, it has a specific timeframe, and it's ambitious enough to require real change. "Improve reading" fails all four. "80% of third-graders will read at grade level by 2028" passes all four.

Clarify Priorities

Our district's goals haven't changed in eight years. Should that concern us?

It depends on whether you've achieved them. Goals that remain because the district keeps hitting them are a sign of effective governance — you set them, you met them, you set new ones. Goals that haven't changed because nobody revisits them are a red flag. Pull the data: are you closer to your goals than you were eight years ago? If you can't easily answer that, the goals aren't doing their job.

Clarify Priorities

What's the difference between a goal and a guardrail?

Goals describe what success looks like for students — the outcomes you're driving toward. Guardrails describe what the district will not do in pursuit of those goals, based on community values. A goal: "90% graduation rate by 2027." A guardrail: "The superintendent shall not implement any program that increases per-pupil expenditure by more than 15% without board approval." Both are the board's job. Neither is management.

How many student outcome goals should a board have?

Three to five. Fewer than three and you risk missing something important; more than five and you've created a sprawl that no one will monitor consistently. The number matters less than the discipline: each goal must be specific, measurable, and actively tracked. If you can't report progress on every goal at your next meeting, you have too many.

How often should the board revisit or revise its student outcome goals?

Review annually, revise strategically. An annual check asks: are the goals still the right goals, are the metrics still meaningful, and has the community's context changed? Major revision should happen every three to five years — enough time for interventions to actually produce results. Changing goals every year in response to political pressure is a sign of a board that doesn't trust its own direction.

Clarify Priorities

What should the board's goal-setting process actually look like?

Start with current student outcome data — not where you want to be, but where you actually are. Layer in community input on what families most need students to know and be able to do. Then draft goals in small group, test them against the SMART criteria, and ratify publicly. The superintendent's job is to advise on feasibility; the board's job is to set the ambition level.

What should a good board monitoring report include?

Three things: the goal being monitored, current performance data compared to the target and to prior periods, and a clear interpretation of whether you're on track, off track, or at risk. Everything else is noise. A monitoring report that requires 45 minutes to present has failed before anyone speaks — good monitoring data should be digestible in under five minutes.

Our superintendent's reports are full of numbers but never tell us whether students are actually improving. What do we ask for?

Ask for a one-page summary for each goal that answers exactly three questions: What is the target? What is the current result? Are we on track? If the summary can't be produced, the data infrastructure isn't built for governance yet. Start there. Your monitoring reports should answer those questions before they hit the board table.

How often should the board formally review progress toward student outcome goals?

At minimum, each goal should be monitored twice per year — once mid-year to enable course correction, and once at year-end to evaluate results. High-stakes goals should be on a quarterly cadence. Build this into a monitoring calendar so it happens by design, not when the board remembers to ask. If it's not scheduled, it won't happen consistently.

Monitor Progress

We review data at every meeting but outcomes never improve. What are we doing wrong?

Data review without consequence isn't monitoring — it's data theater. After seeing results, the board needs to ask one hard question: "What will change?" If the data shows a goal is off track and the answer is "we'll keep watching," the monitoring process has broken down. Real monitoring ends with either a confirmed plan, a changed plan, or a leadership accountability conversation.

How do we build a monitoring calendar that we'll actually follow?

Map each student outcome goal to at least two meeting dates per year. Assign each goal to a specific board member or committee as the accountability lead. Put the calendar in board policy — not just the work plan — so it survives superintendent transitions and new board members. Calendars that exist only in a spreadsheet disappear within eighteen months.

Align Resources

What does it actually mean to align our budget with our student outcome goals?

It means you can draw a line — explicitly, not in theory — between each of your student outcome goals and the programs, staff, and expenditures designed to move those goals. If you can't draw that line in both directions (goal → spending, spending → goal), your budget isn't aligned. Most district budgets are historical documents, not strategic ones. Alignment requires intentionally breaking that pattern.

Align Resources

How involved should the board be in the budget process?

The board sets the strategic direction and approves the budget — it does not build it. Your role is to ask whether the proposed budget advances your student outcome goals and respects your guardrails, then approve or send it back for revision. Line-item scrutiny is management. Asking "show me how this $4M allocation moves our reading goal" is governance.

Align Resources

Our district runs hundreds of programs. How do we know which ones to fund?

Ask two questions of every program: Does it move one of our student outcome goals, and is there evidence it actually works? Programs that can't answer yes to both deserve scrutiny regardless of how long they've existed or how many people love them. Ask your superintendent to build a program inventory that maps each initiative to a goal and an evidence base. If that inventory doesn't exist, create it before next budget season.

How do we handle board members who protect specific budget items for political reasons?

The most effective tool is a shared, explicit standard: we fund what moves our goals. When a protected line item comes up, ask the member to explain how it advances a specific student outcome goal. That question isn't an attack — it's the board doing its job. If the item can't survive that question, the political protection is a governance failure, not a feature.

Align Resources

Our budget process starts before our goals are finalized. How do we fix that timing problem?

Work backwards. If your budget needs to be approved in June, the goals it's designed to advance need to be ratified by October of the prior year. Build your annual governance calendar so goal review precedes budget development — not the other way around. If your goals change every year anyway, the problem is upstream: a board that hasn't committed to a long-range direction will always build budgets in the dark.

Communicate Results

How should a school board communicate student outcome results to the community?

Regularly, plainly, and honestly — even when results are disappointing. The gold standard is a brief annual report that shows each student outcome goal, the current result, and whether you're on track. It should be readable in ten minutes by a parent with no education background. If your community report requires a glossary, it's written for the board, not the community.

Our board reports are too technical for parents to understand. What should we change?

Start by swapping jargon for plain questions: instead of "proficiency rate on state-administered summative assessments," say "how many students are reading on grade level?" Then test your draft with a parent who isn't on any district committee. If they can't explain the results back to you in two sentences, rewrite it. Technical precision matters for staff. Clarity matters for community trust.

How transparent should we be about goals we're not meeting?

Fully transparent. Boards that hide disappointing results lose the community's trust twice — once when the results come out anyway, and again when people learn the board knew. Honest communication about shortfalls, paired with a credible plan to address them, builds far more confidence than silence does. The community doesn't expect perfection; they expect honesty and accountability.

What's the difference between communicating results and doing PR?

PR selects and frames information to make the organization look good. Communicating results shares what the data actually shows — including where goals are being missed. The test is simple: would you communicate this the same way if results were negative? If the answer is no, you're doing PR. Boards that confuse the two eventually face a credibility collapse when reality catches up with the narrative.

Communicate Results

How do we handle negative media coverage about student outcomes?

Don't fight it — respond to it with data and a plan. If the coverage is accurate, acknowledge it directly, share what the board knows, and describe what's changing. If it's inaccurate, correct the specific factual errors without attacking the reporter. Boards that respond to negative coverage defensively tend to make it worse. Boards that respond with transparency and accountability tend to recover faster.

Communicate Results

Should the board communicate outcome results at board meetings, or through other channels?

Both, but for different audiences. Board meetings are where the formal record is made — results should be presented and discussed there. But most community members don't attend board meetings. Annual reports, brief social posts, school newsletters, and town halls reach the people who actually need to understand whether students are making progress. If your only communication channel is the public comment period, you're not actually communicating.
