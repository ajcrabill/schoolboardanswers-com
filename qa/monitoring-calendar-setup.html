--- layout: qa title: "How do we build a monitoring calendar that we'll actually follow?" category: "Monitor Progress" ---

A monitoring calendar that lives in a spreadsheet will be ignored within 18 months. It gets created once, referenced a few times, and quietly abandoned when other things crowd it out. The boards that follow through on monitoring — year after year, regardless of who's on the board or who's in the superintendent chair — do one thing differently: they put monitoring dates in board policy, not just in a planning document.

The goal is to make monitoring a structural governance commitment rather than a good intention. Good intentions are optional. Board policy is not.

Step one: map each goal to at least two meeting dates

Start with the list of student outcome goals the board has adopted. For each goal, assign at least two board meeting dates where that goal will be formally reviewed — one mid-year (October through February) and one year-end (May or June). Spread monitoring across different meetings rather than stacking all goals at the same two dates. A monitoring marathon where five goals get fifteen minutes each is not governance — it's box-checking.

A concrete example for a board with four goals:

  • 3rd grade reading proficiency: November (mid-year check), May (year-end)
  • 8th grade math proficiency: January (mid-year check), May (year-end)
  • Chronic absenteeism: October (early check), February (mid-year), June (year-end)
  • Graduation rate: December (mid-year check), June (year-end)

This distributes monitoring across eight months, keeps individual meetings focused, and ensures no goal disappears for more than five or six months at a stretch.

Step two: assign accountability leads

For each monitoring date, name who is responsible for preparing the report. Not "staff" — a specific person. "The Assistant Superintendent for Instruction will deliver the reading proficiency monitoring report at the November meeting." Named accountability is different from collective responsibility. When everyone is responsible, no one is.

Step three: put it in board policy

This is the step most boards skip, and it's why most monitoring calendars collapse. A spreadsheet calendar depends on whoever created it to maintain it and whoever reads it to remember it exists. Board policy requires compliance. When the monitoring calendar is embedded in governance policy — specifying which goals are monitored, at what minimum frequency, and in what format — it becomes part of the operating system of the board rather than a separate artifact that must be actively tended.

The policy doesn't need to name specific meeting dates (those change year to year). It should specify the minimum monitoring frequency per goal, the required report format, and who is responsible for producing reports. Those parameters are stable across years even as specific dates shift.

The 18-month problem

Boards that rely on spreadsheet calendars typically follow them for one cycle, then hit a disruption — a contested election, a superintendent transition, a budget crisis, a facilities emergency — and monitoring gets bumped. Once it's been bumped once without consequence, it's easy to bump again. Within 18 months, the calendar exists only as a file no one opens.

Policy-embedded monitoring is harder to abandon because abandoning it requires a formal board action. The friction is the point. The board should have to make a conscious decision to skip monitoring — not drift away from it by accident.

When data isn't available on schedule

State assessment results often don't arrive until July or August, after the school year ends. Rather than delaying monitoring until data arrives, use leading indicators at the scheduled date: interim assessment scores, course pass rates, benchmark results, or chronic absenteeism trends. These move before summative data confirms them and still give the board meaningful signal about whether the district is on track.

Document in the monitoring calendar which indicators will be used for each goal at each review point — so staff know what to prepare and the board knows what to expect. The monitoring date stays fixed. The data may shift depending on what's available at that point in the year.
