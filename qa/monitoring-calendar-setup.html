Monitor Progress

"How do we build a monitoring calendar that we'll actually follow?"

← Back to all questions

Map each student outcome goal to at least two meeting dates per year — one mid-year, one at year-end. Put the calendar in board policy, not just the annual work plan, so it survives superintendent transitions and new board members. A monitoring calendar that lives only in a work plan document is one leadership change away from disappearing. A monitoring calendar that is part of board policy is a governance commitment the institution has made to itself.

Start with your goals, not your meeting schedule

The most common mistake in building a monitoring calendar is starting with available meeting slots and fitting goals in around other business. This produces a calendar that looks balanced on paper but gets quietly deprioritized whenever the agenda fills up. Start instead with your student outcome goals and work backward: when does data become available for each goal? How much lead time does staff need to prepare a monitoring report? What is the latest date in the school year at which a mid-year course correction is still actionable?

Answer those questions for each goal, and the calendar largely builds itself. You are not scheduling around convenience — you are scheduling around the data cycle and the window in which the board can still affect outcomes.

What goes into each calendar entry

A monitoring calendar entry is more than a date. For each scheduled review, record:

  • The specific goal being monitored — not "student achievement" but the named, numbered goal from the board's adopted goal set
  • The type of review — mid-year check-in (forward-looking, course-correction focus) or year-end evaluation (summative, accountability focus)
  • The report due date — typically 7–10 days before the meeting so board members can read it in advance
  • The data source — which assessment, which system, which staff member is responsible for producing the numbers
  • Allocated discussion time — block it on the agenda now; 30 minutes minimum per goal for substantive discussion

When these details are established at the beginning of the year, staff know what to prepare and when, board members know what to expect, and there is no ambiguity about whether a monitoring item can be bumped when the agenda gets crowded.

Anchoring the calendar in policy

Work plans are adopted annually and drift. Personnel change. Priorities shift. A monitoring calendar embedded only in the work plan is fragile — it has to be re-established each year and is subject to negotiation each time. Anchoring the monitoring cadence in board policy creates a different dynamic: the requirement to monitor each goal at least twice per year is now an institutional obligation, not a discretionary choice. The work plan then specifies the particular dates and reports within that policy framework.

The policy language doesn't need to be complex. Something as direct as "The board shall formally monitor progress toward each adopted student outcome goal no fewer than twice per year, with at least one review occurring prior to March 1" is sufficient. The superintendent then builds a monitoring schedule that satisfies the policy requirement, and the board evaluates whether the schedule is adequate before the year begins.

What to do when the calendar slips

Even well-designed monitoring calendars get disrupted — a budget crisis, a personnel emergency, an unexpected community issue consumes the agenda. The question is not whether disruptions will happen but how the board responds when they do. Two practices help:

  • Never cancel a monitoring item — reschedule it. If a goal review gets bumped from October, it goes on the November agenda, not into a vague "we'll get to it" queue. The rescheduled date gets recorded in the minutes of the meeting where the bump happened.
  • Track slippage as a board performance indicator. At year-end, review how many monitoring items were completed on schedule versus delayed. Persistent slippage in the same goal area is itself a signal worth examining — is something systematically preventing the board from looking at that data?

A monitoring calendar you actually follow is not the product of good intentions — it is the product of clear structure, policy backing, and a board willing to protect governance time the same way it protects budget adoption and strategic planning. Build the calendar deliberately, anchor it in policy, and treat disruptions as exceptions that require immediate rescheduling rather than quietly absorbed deferrals.
