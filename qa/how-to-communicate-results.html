Communicate Results

"How should a school board communicate student outcome results to the community?"

← Back to all questions

Regularly, plainly, and honestly — even when results are disappointing. The gold standard is a brief annual report that shows each student outcome goal, the current result, and whether you're on track. Most districts communicate results poorly not because they lack data, but because they approach communication as a public relations exercise rather than an accountability obligation. The difference shows. Communities can tell when they're being managed rather than informed, and the trust deficit that follows is harder to recover from than any bad test score.

The structure that works

Effective results communication has a simple architecture. For each adopted student outcome goal, the community should see:

  • The goal itself — stated in plain language, not education jargon. "85% of third graders reading at grade level by 2027" is a goal. "Advancing literacy through multi-tiered systems of support" is not.
  • The current result — the actual number, this year, with the same number from the prior year for context. Don't replace the number with a description of activities. The community wants to know what happened to students, not what staff did.
  • The honest assessment — are you on track, off track, or uncertain? This requires the board to have pre-defined what "on track" looks like, which is itself a governance discipline worth developing.
  • What comes next — a brief, specific statement of what the district will do differently if results are off track, or how it will sustain progress if results are strong.

Frequency and format

At minimum, the board should report results to the community once per year — timed to when annual assessment data is available, typically late summer or early fall. But the annual report should not be the only touchpoint. Boards that communicate results only once a year create a dynamic where good news gets celebrated and bad news gets managed, because both arrive in the same high-stakes annual moment.

More effective is a cadence of lighter-touch interim updates — perhaps twice a year at public board meetings, with results posted to the district website in a format designed for community members, not education insiders. Quarterly data dashboards, brief video updates from the board chair, or a simple email to district families can all serve this function without requiring a major production each time.

The honesty problem

Most boards struggle most with communicating disappointing results. The instinct is to contextualize heavily — to surround bad numbers with explanations, comparisons to state averages, or descriptions of promising early indicators. Some context is legitimate and necessary. But boards that bury the headline, that lead with silver linings and make the community work to find the actual result, are eroding the credibility that makes good communication valuable.

The discipline here is to lead with the result, every time, regardless of whether it's good or bad. "Our third-grade reading rate is 61%, down from 64% last year, and we are not on track to meet our 2027 goal. Here is what we are going to do about it." That sentence is harder to say than a paragraph of context, but it builds more trust over time than any amount of polished PR language.

Who delivers the message matters

The board communicating results is categorically different from district staff communicating results. When the superintendent or communications director presents outcome data, the community receives it as organizational information. When the board chair or full board presents outcome data — especially disappointing results — the community receives it as accountability. The board is saying: we set these goals, we are responsible for whether students meet them, and we are not hiding from the answer.

That posture — the board owning results rather than receiving them from staff — is what distinguishes a governance-focused board from one that treats accountability as a staff function. It signals to the community that someone is actually in charge of outcomes, which is the core thing communities need to believe in their school board.

Building the infrastructure for good communication

None of this is possible without two upstream requirements: goals that are specific enough to be measured, and a data system that can deliver results in a timely and accessible way. Boards that find themselves unable to communicate clearly about results should trace the problem back to one of these two sources. Vague goals produce vague reporting. Data systems that take eight months to produce results create reports that land too late to be actionable. Both are solvable — but they require the board to ask for what it needs from staff, not to accept annual presentations that weren't designed with community accountability in mind.
