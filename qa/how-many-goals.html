Clarify Priorities

"How many student outcome goals should a board have?"

← Back to all questions

Three to five. Fewer than three and you risk missing something important about what students need; more than five and you've created a sprawl that no one will monitor consistently. The number isn't arbitrary — it reflects how much a governing board can actually hold with discipline across a two- to four-year horizon.

Why fewer goals produce more accountability

Most boards that adopt ten or twelve outcome goals don't abandon accountability — they diffuse it. When everything is a priority, nothing is. Board meetings fill up with status updates on initiatives rather than honest reckoning with whether students are making progress on the things that matter most. A shorter list forces a harder, more honest conversation: given everything we could care about, what do we care about most?

Three to five goals also creates a monitoring cadence that's sustainable. If the board commits to reviewing each goal with genuine depth — not a slide deck summary but a real conversation about trend lines and equity gaps — it needs time to do that well. More than five goals and the monitoring calendar becomes impossible to maintain without cutting corners on every review.

What belongs in those three to five goals

The goals should represent the full arc of student preparation, not just the outcomes that are easy to measure. A reasonable set might include:

  • Early literacy — the foundational skill everything else depends on
  • Math proficiency — typically tracked through grade-level benchmarks and course completion
  • Post-secondary readiness — graduation, dual enrollment, college and career indicators
  • Chronic absenteeism or attendance — a leading indicator that cuts across all outcomes
  • An equity-specific measure — disaggregating outcomes by race, income, or disability status to ensure the overall numbers aren't masking gaps

Not every district will use this exact set. What matters is that your goals cover the outcomes your community has identified as essential — and that each goal has a measurable indicator with a specific target and timeline attached.

The trap of adding goals instead of making trade-offs

When a board member raises a legitimate concern — say, student mental health or career-technical education — the reflex is often to add another goal. Resist this. Adding a goal is not the only way to signal that something matters. You can address an important domain through guardrails (how the district treats student wellbeing in pursuing academic goals), through a monitoring sub-indicator nested under an existing goal, or through a superintendent directive. Not everything the board cares about needs to live at the goal level.

If your board is regularly debating whether to add more goals, that's usually a sign that something deeper is missing: community input was too narrow, the goal-setting process didn't invite enough voices, or individual board members feel their constituents aren't represented in the current priorities. The solution is a better process, not a longer list.

Reassessing when the number drifts

Goal count tends to creep upward over time, especially after elections bring new board members who want to add their priorities. Build in an annual discipline: each spring, before the board's goal review, ask explicitly whether every current goal still belongs at the top level. If a goal has been achieved, it should be retired and potentially replaced. If a goal has been consistently met for three years, it may belong in an operational expectation rather than a strategic board-level target. Pruning is as important as adding.
