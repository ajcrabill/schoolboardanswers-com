"How do we redirect a board member who keeps going off-topic?"

← Back to all questions

Off-topic behavior is almost always a symptom of one of two things: unclear agreements about what's in-scope for board discussion, or a member who doesn't feel heard on something they care about. Before reaching for a redirection technique, diagnose which problem you're actually dealing with — because the interventions are different.

First: Is the topic actually out of scope?

Boards that haven't defined what belongs in a board meeting will perpetually argue about it in real time, in public. If your board has no written agreements about what kinds of discussions belong at the board level versus the staff level, then the member going "off-topic" may simply be operating on a different — and not necessarily wrong — mental model of what the board's job is.

The first structural fix is to establish clear norms in writing. This doesn't require a policy overhaul — a one-page board working agreement that describes the types of decisions and discussions appropriate for board meetings is enough to create a shared reference point. Without that, "off-topic" is a matter of opinion, and you'll never win the argument in the moment.

When the member feels unheard

Persistent off-topic behavior is often a signal that someone has a concern they can't get traction on through normal channels. They bring it up sideways, through tangentially related agenda items, because they haven't found another path. This is a relationship and process problem masquerading as a decorum problem.

The board chair or a fellow member should have a direct one-on-one conversation with this person outside of the meeting. Ask them what they're trying to accomplish. What do they feel isn't getting enough attention? What outcome do they want? Often this conversation surfaces a legitimate governance concern that hasn't had a proper forum — and once it does, the off-topic behavior drops significantly. If the concern is valid, find it a home on a future agenda. If it's outside the board's scope, explain why clearly and with respect.

In-meeting redirection techniques that actually work

When redirection is needed in the moment, the chair's approach matters enormously. Techniques that tend to work:

  • Name and park, don't dismiss. "That's worth discussing — let me add it to the parking lot so we can come back to it after we finish the current item." This acknowledges the concern without derailing the meeting, and commits to returning to it.
  • Refer back to the agenda purpose. "I want to make sure we finish this item — can we hold that thought?" This frames the redirect as time management, not dismissal.
  • Ask a bridging question. "How does that connect to the decision we're trying to make tonight?" This puts the burden on the member to draw the connection rather than making the chair seem arbitrary. Often the member will self-redirect when pressed to articulate the relevance.
  • Use the board norms. If you have written working agreements, reference them directly and matter-of-factly. "Our norms say we table items that come up outside the agenda for a future meeting — let's do that here." This depersonalizes the redirect entirely.

What the board chair should not do

Avoid two common traps. The first is letting the redirect slide because confrontation feels uncomfortable — this trains the board that off-topic behavior has no real cost, and guarantees more of it. Every time a chair allows a tangent to run for fifteen minutes, the implicit message is that the agenda is negotiable and whoever pushes hardest controls the meeting.

The second trap is publicly shaming or abruptly cutting off a member in a way that escalates rather than redirects. That approach damages trust and often produces a defensive response that consumes more time than the original tangent would have. Redirection should be calm, consistent, and impersonal — applied the same way to every member, every time, so no one can claim they were singled out.

The longer-term fix

If a single member's off-topic behavior is a recurring pattern despite one-on-one conversation and consistent in-meeting redirection, the board has a governance maturity issue that needs to be addressed collectively. A facilitated board retreat — ideally with an external facilitator who can name dynamics that internal members won't — can accomplish in a half-day what internal conversations have failed to resolve in months. The investment is worth it when the alternative is years of meetings where the board never gets to the work that actually matters for students.
