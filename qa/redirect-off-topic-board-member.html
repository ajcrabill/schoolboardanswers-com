--- layout: qa title: "How do we redirect a board member who keeps going off-topic?" category: "Focus Mindset" ---

Off-topic behavior is almost always a symptom of one of two things: unclear agreements about what belongs in a board meeting, or a member who doesn't feel heard on something they care about. Before reaching for a redirection technique, diagnose which problem you're actually dealing with — because the interventions are different.

First: Is the topic actually out of scope?

Your board's work has a specific scope: student outcome goals, guardrails, and anything legally required of the board. Everything else — operational decisions, staffing, program implementation — belongs to the superintendent. If your board hasn't made this distinction explicit in writing, then a member raising "off-topic" issues may simply be operating on a different mental model of what the board's job is. That's a clarity problem, not a decorum problem.

The first structural fix is to establish clear norms in writing. A one-page board working agreement that defines the types of decisions and discussions appropriate for board meetings is enough to create a shared reference point. Without it, "off-topic" is a matter of opinion, and you'll never win that argument in the moment.

When the member feels unheard

Persistent off-topic behavior is often a signal that someone has a concern they can't get traction on through normal channels. They bring it up sideways, through tangentially related agenda items, because they haven't found another path. This is a relationship and process problem masquerading as a decorum problem.

The board chair or a fellow member should have a direct one-on-one conversation with this person outside of the meeting. Ask them what they're trying to accomplish. What do they feel isn't getting enough attention? What outcome do they want? Often this conversation surfaces a legitimate governance concern that hasn't had a proper forum — and once it does, the off-topic behavior drops. If the concern is valid, find it a home on a future agenda. If it's outside the board's scope, explain why clearly and with respect.

In-meeting redirection techniques that actually work

When redirection is needed in the moment, the chair's approach matters enormously. Techniques that tend to work:

  • Name and park, don't dismiss. "That's worth discussing — let me add it to the parking lot so we can come back to it after we finish the current item." This acknowledges the concern without derailing the meeting, and commits to returning to it.
  • Refer back to the agenda purpose. "I want to make sure we finish this item — can we hold that thought?" This frames the redirect as time management, not dismissal.
  • Ask a bridging question. "How does that connect to the decision we're trying to make tonight?" This puts the burden on the member to draw the connection rather than making the chair seem arbitrary. Often the member will self-redirect when pressed to articulate the relevance.
  • Use the board norms. If your board has written working agreements, reference them directly and matter-of-factly. "Our norms say we table items that come up outside the agenda for a future meeting — let's do that here." This depersonalizes the redirect entirely.

What the board chair should not do

Avoid two common traps. The first is letting the redirect slide because confrontation feels uncomfortable — this trains your board that off-topic behavior has no real cost, and guarantees more of it. Every time a chair allows a tangent to run for fifteen minutes, the implicit message is that the agenda is negotiable and whoever pushes hardest controls the meeting.

The second trap is publicly shaming or abruptly cutting off a member in a way that escalates rather than redirects. That approach damages trust and often produces a defensive response that consumes more time than the original tangent would have. Redirection should be calm, consistent, and impersonal — applied the same way to every member, every time, so no one can claim they were singled out.

The longer-term fix

If a single member's off-topic behavior is a recurring pattern despite one-on-one conversation and consistent in-meeting redirection, your board has a governance maturity issue that needs to be addressed collectively. A facilitated board retreat — ideally with an external facilitator who can name dynamics that internal members won't — can accomplish in a half-day what internal conversations have failed to resolve in months. The investment is worth it when the alternative is years of meetings where your board never gets to the work that actually matters for students.

Practical steps

  1. Write down what belongs in a board meeting. Your board's working agreements should explicitly state that board discussions are scoped to student outcome goals, guardrails, and legally required board functions. Post it in every meeting packet. A member who goes off-topic after that agreement exists is raising a different kind of problem than one who does it in the absence of any shared definition.
  2. Teach the board chair to redirect early, not late. A tangent is easier to redirect after thirty seconds than after five minutes. The chair's job is to interrupt politely before the discussion has momentum — "Let me park that and keep us on the current item" — not to wait until the meeting has gone sideways.
  3. Have the one-on-one conversation before the pattern solidifies. When a member raises something off-topic once, note it. When they do it twice, have the direct conversation outside of the meeting. Ask what they're trying to accomplish. Find a legitimate path for the concern if one exists, or explain clearly why it doesn't belong at the board level.
  4. Build a parking lot into every meeting structure. A standing parking lot item at the end of each meeting agenda gives the chair a natural destination for off-topic items. It signals that concerns won't be lost — just held until the right time. Members who know their issue will surface eventually are less likely to force it into an unrelated agenda item.
  5. Address persistent patterns at a board retreat, not in a board meeting. If one-on-one conversations and in-meeting redirection haven't resolved the behavior, bring in an external facilitator for a governance session. Name the pattern directly, connect it to its impact on the board's ability to do its goal and guardrail work, and reach explicit agreements about how the board will operate going forward.
