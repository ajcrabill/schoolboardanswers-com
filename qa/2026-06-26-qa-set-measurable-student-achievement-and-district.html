How can we set measurable student achievement and district performance targets to hold the superintendent accountable for results, not just effort?

June 23, 2026
  1. Your board holds the superintendent accountable for results by defining the outcomes you want, identifying the indicators the superintendent can actually influence, and scheduling regular reviews where data replaces narrative.
  1. The principle is simple: effort is not the same as results. Your board should care about what students can do, not whether the superintendent attended enough meetings. This distinction matters because the superintendent controls instructional decisions, staffing, and resource allocation. Those choices drive student outcomes. Your job is to track whether those choices produce the results you promised taxpayers.
  1. The common mistake is confusing compliance with progress. Many boards monitor whether the superintendent completed a list of initiatives. That tells you about activity, not impact. The right approach is to set targets tied to actual student achievement data. For example, instead of "implement a new reading program," your target becomes "third-grade reading proficiency rises from 52% to 62% within 18 months." You can measure the second one. The first one only tells you something was done.
  1. In practice, this looks like your board agreeing on three to five district-wide outcome targets each year. These targets must be specific enough to measure. They must connect to data your district already collects. They must reflect what the superintendent can actually move through budget decisions, staffing, and instructional priorities. When you review progress, you look at the numbers. You ask what the numbers mean. You ask what the superintendent will do differently if the numbers fall short.
  1. One honest complication deserves direct attention: external factors affect student outcomes in ways the superintendent cannot control. State funding cuts, family instability, and community health crises all influence results. Your board should acknowledge these constraints when setting targets. Build in realistic timelines. But do not use external factors as a reason to avoid setting targets altogether. The superintendent controls the district's response to those factors. Your targets should reflect what a well-run district can achieve, not what an ideal world would produce.
  1. Here are the steps:

- Choose one student outcome target for the next school year. Pick something your district can measure with existing assessments.

- Define what success looks like in concrete numbers. "Reading proficiency at grade level" is not a number. "63% of eighth-graders meet or exceed state reading standards" is a number.

- At your next one-on-one with the superintendent, ask directly: "What would you need from the board to make this goal achievable?" Listen for whether the answer involves resources you control or changes in authority.

- Schedule a quarterly data review on your board calendar. Do not wait for the superintendent to bring data. Your agenda should include the target, the current measurement, and a superintendent response to any gap.

- Hold the target for at least one full academic cycle before adjusting it. Changing targets mid-year signals that your board does not mean what it says. Consistency builds accountability over time.
