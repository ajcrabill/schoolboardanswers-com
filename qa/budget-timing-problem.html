--- layout: qa title: "Our budget process starts before our goals are finalized. How do we fix that timing problem?" category: "Align Resources" ---

Work backwards from your budget approval date. If the board adopts the budget in June, the goals it's designed to advance need to be formally ratified no later than October of the prior year. That's not a preference — it's a structural requirement. A budget built before goals are set is a budget built in the dark, organized around last year's assumptions rather than this year's strategic direction.

Most boards that face this timing problem didn't create it deliberately. The budget calendar is driven by external deadlines — state submission dates, fiscal year requirements, staff contracting timelines. The goal-setting calendar is softer and more negotiable, so it tends to give way. Over years, the two processes drift further apart until no one can remember a time when goals came first.

The annual governance calendar that puts goals first

For a district with a June budget adoption, the properly sequenced calendar looks like this:

  1. August–September: Board reviews student outcome data from the prior school year. What improved? What didn't? Where are the gaps? This is the evidence base for goal decisions.
  2. October: Board formally ratifies student outcome goals for the coming budget cycle. These goals become the brief the superintendent takes into budget development. This step must conclude with a board vote — goals discussed but never formally adopted don't count.
  3. November: Superintendent returns with a resource allocation framework: given the ratified goals, here is how we propose to organize spending priorities for the coming year. Board responds with guidance before drafting begins.
  4. January–March: Budget is built by staff using the ratified goals and board guidance as the organizing frame. Departments develop requests that connect to goals rather than simply replicating prior-year allocations.
  5. April–May: Superintendent presents the proposed budget to the board with explicit goal-alignment documentation. Board reviews and asks questions.
  6. June: Board adopts the budget.

The upstream problem: boards that change goals annually

Some boards face a timing problem not because their calendar is wrong but because their goals are perpetually unsettled. When the board revisits and revises its goals every year — responding to new board members, new community pressures, new political priorities — the goal-setting process never really concludes. It bleeds into budget season and the two processes collide.

This pattern has a name: annual goal churn. And it's more damaging than the timing problem it creates. Districts that shift goals every year cannot build coherent multi-year strategies. Staff learn to wait out the goal-setting debate rather than act on it. Budget alignment becomes impossible because alignment requires a stable target.

The multi-year goal as the solution

The most effective fix for chronic timing misalignment is multi-year goals. When the board adopts goals with a three-to-five year horizon — "80% of students reading proficiently by grade 3 by 2028" — the goal doesn't need to be relitigated each fall. Annual review confirms whether the goal still makes sense and updates the interim targets, but doesn't restart the goal-setting process from scratch.

Multi-year goals also make budget alignment possible over time. A district can build a coherent resource strategy around a three-year goal in a way it simply cannot around a goal that might change next October. Stability in goals enables coherence in strategy, which enables alignment in budgets.

How to hold the October deadline

The biggest obstacle to fixing the timing problem isn't technical — it's that goal-setting is genuinely difficult. It surfaces real disagreements about priorities, equity, and community values that budget debates often don't. Boards that consistently miss the October ratification deadline are usually boards that haven't resolved those underlying disagreements.

If that's your situation, the work isn't to fix the calendar — it's to address the disagreement directly. A board retreat in late summer, facilitated by someone who can surface and work through value conflicts, is often more useful than another goal-setting work session that produces the same impasse in October.

And if you're mid-cycle right now, with goals unfinished and a budget process already underway: ratify whatever goals you can agree on now, even if they're incomplete. An explicit, board-adopted goal statement that arrives in February is better than a comprehensive framework that arrives in May after the major spending decisions are locked. Imperfect goals that exist are more useful than perfect goals that are still being negotiated.
