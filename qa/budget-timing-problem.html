"Our budget process starts before our goals are finalized. How do we fix that timing problem?"

← Back to all questions

Work backwards. If your budget needs to be approved in June, the goals it's designed to advance need to be ratified by October of the prior year. That's not a suggestion — it's a structural requirement. A budget built before goals are set is a budget built on assumptions about what the district is trying to accomplish. Sometimes those assumptions are right. More often, the budget ends up reflecting what the district did last year, dressed up in forward-looking language that was added after the fact.

Why the timing problem exists

The misalignment between goal-setting and budget-building is almost never deliberate. It's a product of how each process evolved independently. Budgets follow the fiscal year and state deadlines — they have hard external constraints. Goal-setting follows board election cycles, strategic planning timelines, and the rhythms of community engagement processes — and those constraints are softer and more negotiable. Over time, the budget process claimed its calendar and the goal-setting process worked around it, which means goals routinely arrive too late to actually shape the budget.

The result is a district that talks about being outcomes-focused but allocates resources by inertia. Departments build their requests based on what they got last year. Central office aggregates those requests. The superintendent presents the result. The board approves it. And at no point does anyone ask whether the total picture advances the district's stated student outcome goals — because those goals either don't exist yet, or they were finalized after the major allocation decisions were already made.

The backward-design calendar

Fixing this requires treating goal ratification as a budget prerequisite — a hard constraint, not a soft aspiration. Here is what a properly sequenced annual governance calendar looks like for a district with a June budget adoption:

  1. September–October: Board reviews student outcome data from the prior year and reaffirms or revises its goals. This work should conclude with a formal board action ratifying the goals that will guide the upcoming budget.
  2. November: Superintendent presents the board with a resource allocation framework — a description of how the coming budget will be structured to advance each goal. The board responds with guidance before any detailed drafting begins.
  3. January–March: Budget is drafted by staff with the ratified goals and board guidance as the explicit brief. Departments build requests that connect to goals rather than simply replicating last year.
  4. April–May: Superintendent presents the proposed budget to the board with explicit goal-alignment documentation. Board reviews, asks questions, and provides final direction.
  5. June: Board adopts the budget.

The hardest part: holding the October deadline

The biggest obstacle to this sequence isn't technical — it's political. Goal-setting is harder and more contentious than budget approval for most boards. It requires actual agreement on what the district is trying to accomplish, which surfaces real disagreements among board members about priorities, equity, and community values. Budget debates often look contentious but are mostly about numbers; goal debates are about direction.

Boards that consistently miss the October goal-ratification deadline are usually boards that haven't resolved their disagreements about goals. The timing problem is a symptom of a deeper governance problem. If that's the situation, the board needs to address the underlying disagreement directly rather than managing it by perpetually delaying goal adoption.

A transition year approach

If your board is currently mid-cycle with goals unfinished and a budget process already underway, you can't fully fix the sequence this year — but you can take two steps. First, ratify your goals as quickly as possible, even if they're incomplete or provisional. An explicit, board-adopted statement of goals that arrives in February is better than a comprehensive goal framework that arrives in May. Second, ask the superintendent to map the current draft budget against those provisional goals and report back on the gaps. That exercise may not change this year's budget significantly, but it establishes the practice and makes the misalignment visible. Visible problems get fixed; invisible ones don't.
