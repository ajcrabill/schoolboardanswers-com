Align Resources

"What does it actually mean to align our budget with our student outcome goals?"

← Back to all questions

Alignment means you can draw an explicit line — not a theoretical one, not an assumed one — between each of your student outcome goals and the specific programs, staffing decisions, and expenditures designed to move those goals. It works in both directions: goal to dollars, and dollars back to goal.

If you can't draw those lines, your budget isn't aligned. It may be a perfectly reasonable budget assembled with good intentions. But it's a historical document — a record of what the district has always funded — not a strategic one.

What assumed alignment looks like

Most districts assume alignment rather than demonstrating it. The reasoning goes something like this: "We care about reading, we fund the reading curriculum, therefore the budget aligns with our reading goal." That's not alignment — that's optimism. Real alignment requires knowing which specific expenditures are tied to each goal, what each dollar is supposed to produce in student outcomes, and how you'll know if it's working.

The two-direction test

Before your board approves any budget, run it through two questions:

  • Goal to dollars: For each of your student outcome goals, can you identify the specific budget lines funding the work designed to move that goal? If not, either the goal is unfunded or the connection is invisible.
  • Dollars to goal: For your largest expenditure categories, can you trace them back to a specific student outcome goal? If a major line item can't be connected to any goal, ask why the district is funding it.

Why most district budgets fail this test

District budgets are built incrementally. This year's budget starts with last year's budget and makes adjustments. Over time, programs accumulate that reflect past priorities, contractual obligations, political commitments, and inertia — not the board's current student outcome goals. Breaking that pattern requires intentional work. The superintendent builds the budget, but the board sets the expectation that alignment must be demonstrated, not assumed.

Making alignment visible

Ask your superintendent to build a simple program inventory that maps each significant initiative to: (1) the student outcome goal it serves, (2) the evidence that it works, and (3) the budget allocation it receives. Focus on the top 80% of spending by dollar amount.

When that inventory exists, budget alignment becomes reviewable rather than asserted. The board can ask: "Show us how the $3.2 million literacy initiative connects to our third-grade reading goal and what we expect it to produce." That question is governance. The answer — or the inability to answer it — tells you everything about whether your resources are aligned to your priorities.

The board's role

The board doesn't build the budget — the superintendent does. But the board creates the conditions for alignment by establishing a standing expectation: every significant expenditure must trace to a student outcome goal, and the board will ask that question at budget approval. When that expectation is consistent, staff learn to build budgets that way. When it's absent, they don't.
