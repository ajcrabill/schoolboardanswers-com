How should a board member navigate the conflict between campaign promises about a specific curriculum and their fiduciary duty to evaluate vendor contracts and student outcomes impartially?

# Campaign Promises and Fiduciary Duty

Your board's role is to set direction, not to run the implementation. When campaign promises point toward a specific curriculum, your duty is to evaluate whether that curriculum serves student outcomes—not to defend or attack the promise itself. The conflict you feel is real, but the answer is simple: separate the political commitment from the governance decision. Your board's fiduciary duty is to students first, always.

The principle at work here is board authority versus board execution. Your board has the power to set goals, approve budgets, and hold the superintendent accountable for results. Your board does not have the power—or the expertise—to select curriculum vendors, design instructional materials, or manage implementation. Campaign promises about a specific curriculum are political commitments made by individuals. Governance decisions about curriculum are institutional commitments made by the board as a body. Conflating them is where most boards get into trouble.

The common mistake is treating the campaign promise as a board directive. A board member who campaigned on "implementing Reading Rainbow curriculum" may feel bound to push that specific program once elected. But the promise was made outside the governance structure. Once in office, your board's obligation is to the district's stated goals: student achievement, fiscal responsibility, legal compliance. If a vendor contract for that curriculum aligns with those goals, approve it. If it does not, reject it. The promise you made to voters is not the same as the promise you make to students.

What this looks like in practice is straightforward. When the curriculum vendor proposal arrives, your board reviews it the same way it reviews every vendor proposal: Does this contract serve the district's adopted goals? What does the monitoring data say about projected student outcomes? Is the cost reasonable compared to alternatives? Your board should not ask "Does this fulfill our campaign promise?" Your board should ask "Does this serve our students?" The first question is political. The second is governance. Your role is to answer the second, and to let the political layer take care of itself.

One honest complication: what if the campaign promise was specifically about student outcomes? Sometimes candidates promise "better reading scores" or "science proficiency by grade five" and tie those goals to a specific curriculum. In this case, your board's job is to hold the superintendent to the outcome goal—not to the specific curriculum. The superintendent manages implementation. Your board monitors results. If the promised curriculum fails to produce the promised outcomes, your board's fiduciary duty is to demand a different approach, regardless of who championed the original plan. This is not betrayal of a promise. This is fulfillment of your duty.

Practical steps:

  1. At your next goal-setting workshop, ensure every board-adopted goal is stated in terms of student outcomes, not vendor choices. If a goal names a specific curriculum, revise it to name the result you want.
  1. When a vendor contract comes before your board, ask this question: "What monitoring data will show whether this curriculum is working?" If the superintendent cannot answer, delay the vote.
  1. In your board communications, separate your political history from your governance role. Say "I campaigned on improving reading outcomes" rather than "I campaigned on adopting Reading Rainbow." The first frames your intent. The second frames your demand.
  1. At your next one-on-one with the superintendent, ask directly: "What would you need from the board to make this outcome goal achievable?" This invites collaboration rather than dictation.
  1. Recognize that this tension does not end. Every election cycle brings new promises. Your board's discipline is to convert every promise into a measurable outcome goal, then evaluate every vendor contract against that goal alone. This is how you serve students consistently, regardless of who made what promise.
