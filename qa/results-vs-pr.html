Communicate Results

"What's the difference between communicating results and doing PR?"

← Back to all questions

PR selects and frames information to make the organization look good. Communicating results shares what the data actually shows — including where goals are being missed. The distinction sounds simple, but in practice it collapses easily. Most school boards that are doing PR don't think of it that way; they think of it as "positive communication" or "celebrating wins." The test isn't intent — it's whether a community member reading your communications would come away with an accurate picture of student outcomes.

The structural difference

PR starts with a desired impression and selects facts that support it. Results communication starts with the data and reports what it shows. That order of operations matters enormously. A board doing PR might accurately report that third-grade reading scores improved by 3 points — while omitting that the district is still 18 points below the state average and that the improvement is within the margin of error. Every individual fact is true; the overall picture is false.

Results communication would report the 3-point improvement in the context of where the district stands relative to its own goals, relative to the state, and relative to where it was five years ago. That full picture may be less flattering, but it's what the community needs to exercise meaningful oversight.

Common PR patterns to watch for in your own communications

Most boards slide into PR gradually, through small editorial choices that seem reasonable in isolation. Watch for these patterns:

  • Leading with the best subgroup. Reporting that "our advanced learners showed strong growth" while burying results for students who are struggling is selective framing.
  • Comparing to a favorable baseline. Choosing the year of a historical low as your comparison point makes improvement look larger than it is.
  • Announcing programs, not outcomes. "We launched a new reading curriculum this year" describes an input. It tells the community nothing about whether students are reading better.
  • Using effort as evidence of progress. "Our teachers worked incredibly hard" may be true, but it's not a student outcome.
  • Omitting goals entirely. Reporting results without reference to the goals the board set makes it impossible for anyone to assess whether performance is adequate.

Why boards drift toward PR

The drift usually isn't cynical. Boards face real pressure from staff who are proud of their work, from community members who prefer good news, and from political dynamics that punish candor. When a board member genuinely believes in the school system and genuinely wants families to feel confident, the instinct to emphasize what's working is natural. The problem is that it prioritizes institutional comfort over the community's ability to make informed decisions about a public institution that serves their children.

There's also a competence question at play. Boards that have not set clear, measurable outcome goals have no benchmark against which to evaluate results — so "communicating results" defaults to sharing whatever data looks favorable, because there's no defined standard for what would count as success or failure.

The governance test

A useful internal check: after reviewing any planned communication, ask whether someone who read only this document would understand where the district is falling short. If the answer is no, the communication is doing PR regardless of whether every individual claim is technically accurate. The community's ability to hold the board accountable depends on having an accurate picture. Communications that obscure that picture — even through omission — undermine the board's basic democratic function.

PR has a place — just not where accountability belongs

This is not an argument against positive communications altogether. Celebrating a teacher of the year, announcing a new partnership, or sharing a student success story are all legitimate uses of a district's communications capacity. The problem arises when those communications crowd out honest outcome reporting, or when they're used to manage perception during periods of poor performance. Keep them separate: results reporting is a governance function; celebration is a community-building function. Both matter, but they should not be conflated.
