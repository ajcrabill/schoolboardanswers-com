Communicate Results

"How do we handle negative media coverage about student outcomes?"

← Back to all questions

Don't fight it — respond to it with data and a plan. If the coverage is accurate, acknowledge it directly, share what the board knows, and describe what's changing. Fighting accurate negative coverage makes the board look defensive and out of touch. It also extends the story, because conflict is more newsworthy than the original data. A board that responds with candor and a credible response usually ends the cycle faster and comes out with more credibility than it started with.

First, assess whether the coverage is accurate

Before responding, the board needs to determine whether the reporting is factually correct. This is not about whether the framing feels fair — it's about whether the underlying facts are right. If a story says 40% of your students are reading below grade level and that number is accurate, the story is accurate regardless of what context might surround it. If specific numbers are wrong or the story contains material factual errors, those are worth correcting directly and specifically. But "this doesn't capture everything we're doing" is not a factual correction — it's a reframing attempt, and reporters and readers recognize the difference.

When the coverage is accurate: respond with ownership

A board that responds to accurate negative coverage by acknowledging the results, explaining its understanding of why, and naming specific actions underway accomplishes several things at once. It signals to the community that the board is aware of the problem and taking it seriously. It signals to the reporter that the board is a reliable source of honest information — which shapes future coverage. And it shifts the story from "bad results" to "board responding to bad results," which is a meaningfully different and more manageable narrative.

The response should include:

  • Acknowledgment that the results are not acceptable
  • What the board understands about the contributing factors (without deflecting blame entirely onto external forces)
  • Specific, named actions the board and administration are taking in response
  • A timeline for when the board expects to see a measurable change — and a commitment to report back publicly on that

When the coverage contains errors: correct precisely

If there are factual errors, contact the reporter or editor directly, cite the correct data with a source, and request a correction. Be specific — "the graduation rate cited in the story was 67%; the actual figure from our state report card is 71%" is a correction. "The story doesn't represent the full picture" is not. Corrections that stick are narrow and verifiable. Broad complaints about framing rarely produce retractions and often produce follow-up stories about whether the board is being forthcoming.

What not to do

Several common board responses to negative coverage reliably make things worse:

  • Attacking the reporter or outlet. This is almost never productive and frequently becomes the story itself.
  • Issuing a statement full of programs and inputs. Responding to "test scores are down" with "we've launched three new initiatives" sounds like deflection because it is. Programs are not outcomes.
  • Going silent. "No comment" or failing to respond signals the board has something to hide, or doesn't take the question seriously.
  • Having the superintendent respond while the board says nothing. The board is the governing authority. On matters of student outcomes, board leadership should be visible and accountable — not hiding behind staff.

The best protection against damaging coverage is proactive disclosure

Boards that regularly and honestly communicate student outcome data — including shortfalls — are rarely blindsided by negative coverage, because there's nothing new for the media to reveal. When the board has already told the community that reading scores are below target and described what it's doing in response, a story reporting the same data has far less impact. The story has already been told by the board itself, in its own voice, with its own context. Proactive transparency is not just ethically right; it's the most effective media strategy available to a board.
