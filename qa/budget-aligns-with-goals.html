How do we know if our budget aligns with our goals?

← Back to all questions

Most district budgets look like last year's budget with small adjustments. That's not a budget aligned to goals — that's a historical document wearing a current-year label. If your board can't trace a clear line from your student outcome goals to the dollars you're approving, you don't actually know whether your spending is advancing anything that matters.

The good news: you can test this. It takes some work, but the test itself is straightforward.

The mapping test

Run it in both directions. First, take each of your board-adopted goals and ask: where in the budget is this funded? If you can't point to a specific line, program, or allocation, that goal is aspirational at best. Second, take your largest spending categories and ask: which board goal does this advance? If the answer is "general operations" for everything, your budget isn't a strategy — it's a maintenance plan.

Both directions have to work. Goal-to-spending reveals what you say you care about but aren't funding. Spending-to-goal reveals what you're actually funding regardless of what you say you care about.

Why most district budgets are historical documents

Budgets are built incrementally. Programs that were added five years ago for reasons no one quite remembers are still there. Staffing patterns reflect last decade's priorities. Central office allocations have drifted from any articulated strategy. No one set out to misalign the budget — it just accumulated.

That's how a district can sincerely say "literacy is our top priority" while the budget shows that most new spending went somewhere else entirely. The misalignment isn't dishonest. It's structural. And it won't fix itself without deliberate pressure from the board.

The quick audit to run

You don't need a full program review to get started. Ask your superintendent to bring a one-page document to the next budget work session that shows:

  • Each board goal listed at the top
  • The three to five largest spending categories under each goal
  • Total dollars attributed to each goal
  • Percentage of discretionary budget — excluding fixed costs — mapped to each goal

If this document doesn't exist and can't be produced quickly, that's itself an answer. A district that cannot map its spending to its goals on request has not built a goal-aligned budget.

Red flags to watch for

  • Large budget lines — professional development, technology, central office staffing — with no stated goal connection
  • Programs that appear in every year's budget but never appear in any board discussion about student outcomes
  • A budget narrative that lists goals but never shows spending against them
  • Discretionary spending concentrated in areas not named in board goals
  • A budget that looks identical in structure to the one from three years ago, despite goals that changed

What to ask at budget approval

Before voting to approve, ask three questions:

  1. Can you show me how the new or increased spending in this budget directly connects to our adopted goals?
  2. Are there any goals that received less funding this year than last — and if so, why?
  3. What spending in this budget could we not connect to a student outcome goal if we tried?

You're not interrogating the superintendent. You're establishing the expectation that spending requires a rationale tied to goals — and that the board will check.

Next steps when alignment is weak

Don't reject the budget on the spot — that creates a crisis without a solution. Instead, approve with a clear directive: before next year's budget process begins, the superintendent will bring a program inventory that maps current spending to current goals, with a recommendation for how to strengthen alignment over the next two to three budget cycles. Then hold to that timeline.

Strategic alignment rarely happens in one year. What matters is that you've created the expectation it's the standard — and that you'll track progress against it.
