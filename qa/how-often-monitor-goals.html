Monitor Progress

How often should the board formally review progress toward student outcome goals?

← Back to topics

At minimum, each goal should be formally reviewed twice per year: once mid-year to enable course correction, and once at year-end to evaluate results. Two monitoring cycles is the floor, not the target. The floor exists because a single year-end review tells you what happened but can't change it. The mid-year review is where governance actually does work.

Two reviews per year is sustainable for any board. The boards that resist it usually have the same problem: monitoring isn't built into the calendar, so it gets crowded out by everything else that is.

Why mid-year matters more than year-end

Year-end monitoring is mostly retrospective. It closes the loop, informs the superintendent evaluation, and sets the baseline for next year's goals. That's valuable — but by June, the school year is over. Nothing can change for the students who experienced it.

The mid-year review is where the board still has leverage. If February data shows a goal is off track, there are still three or four months of the school year left. That's enough time to request a modified approach, shift resources, or signal that a course change is needed. A board that only monitors at year-end has no mechanism to influence what happens to students during the year. It can only evaluate what already occurred.

When to monitor more frequently

Some goals warrant more than two reviews per year:

  • High-stakes goals — goals tied to state accountability thresholds, accreditation requirements, or grant conditions — should be reviewed quarterly so problems surface early enough to address
  • Goals with real-time data — chronic absenteeism, course failure rates, and suspension rates produce data monthly or better; quarterly monitoring is feasible and high-value for these
  • Goals that were off track last year — a goal that missed its target deserves closer attention until the trend reverses
  • Equity goals — closing gaps between student subgroups tends to require consistent attention because progress is uneven and easy to lose in aggregated data

Why consistency matters more than frequency

A board that monitors every goal twice per year, reliably, on a pre-set schedule, will outperform a board that monitors sporadically at higher frequency. Consistency creates several things that irregular monitoring cannot:

  • Staff preparation time — when monitoring dates are known in advance, reports arrive ready rather than rushed
  • Board continuity — members can track trends across cycles rather than encountering data cold each time
  • Accountability weight — a monitoring date on the calendar that everyone knows is coming carries more governance force than an improvised check-in

What happens when monitoring isn't scheduled

Without a monitoring calendar, goal review becomes dependent on whether the superintendent remembers to bring it up, whether a board member asks, or whether a crisis makes avoidance impossible. None of those is a governance system. They're all reactive, and reactive monitoring mostly happens after problems become undeniable — which is too late for course correction.

How to distribute monitoring across the year

Don't schedule all goals for the same two meetings. If the board has five student outcome goals and schedules all five for the February meeting and the June meeting, those meetings become marathons and each goal gets inadequate attention. Instead, spread monitoring across the year:

  • Assign two or three goals to November or December (early mid-year check)
  • Assign the remaining goals to February or March (late mid-year check)
  • Schedule all goals for May or June (year-end review)

This distributes the cognitive load, keeps individual meetings manageable, and ensures no goal goes more than five or six months without formal board review. Set the schedule in August or September before the year begins, and record it in the monitoring calendar as a binding governance commitment — not a suggestion.
