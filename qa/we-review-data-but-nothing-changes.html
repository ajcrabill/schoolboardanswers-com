--- layout: qa title: "We review data at every meeting but outcomes never improve. What are we doing wrong?" category: "Monitor Progress" ---

Data review without consequence is data theater. The pattern looks like governance but produces none of its effects: the board receives a presentation, asks a few questions about methodology or a particular subgroup, notes whether numbers are up or down from last year, and moves on. Nothing is required. Nothing changes. The cycle repeats next quarter. If outcomes aren't improving, the failure almost always lives in what the board does — or doesn't do — after the data lands.

The problem isn't that the board is looking at data. The problem is that looking at data is where the governance work stops.

The critical question after every data review

At the end of every monitoring discussion where results fall short of the board's goals, one question must be asked aloud and answered on the record: "What will change?" Not "What is already being done?" — that question invites a summary of existing programs, which produces reassurance rather than accountability. "What will change?" demands a forward commitment. It requires the superintendent to name a specific adjustment — to strategy, resources, staffing, or focus — and go on record with it. Then the board has something to check at the next monitoring cycle.

Boards that skip this question are implicitly accepting whatever results they receive. Administrators learn — reasonably — that off-track results require a good explanation but not a different approach. The loop never closes, and outcomes drift.

Three possible responses to off-track data

When monitoring shows results below the board's adopted targets, exactly three responses are appropriate:

  1. Confirmed plan: The superintendent explains why the current plan remains adequate and what evidence supports that judgment. The board acknowledges this on the record and schedules the next monitoring date. This is the right response when early results are below target but the trajectory is solid.
  2. Changed plan: The superintendent identifies what specifically will be different — different approach, different resource allocation, different timeline — and commits to a follow-up report by a specific date. The board records the commitment and the follow-up date in the minutes. This is the right response when the current approach is not producing results.
  3. Leadership accountability conversation: The board and superintendent discuss whether the gap between goal and results has become a leadership performance issue requiring formal attention. This is appropriate when results are persistently off track and prior commitments to change have not been fulfilled.

Every monitoring discussion that identifies off-track results should end with one of these three responses, explicitly named, recorded, and scheduled for follow-up. If a meeting ends without one of them, the board has done data theater.

The difference between reviewing data and governing with data

Reviewing data means receiving information. Governing with data means making decisions and demands based on it. A board governs with data when it uses results to direct the superintendent's work, allocate resources, revise goals, and evaluate leadership. Most boards stop at receiving. The shift to governing requires the board to internalize that its job is not to understand the data — it's to require the right outcomes and hold leadership accountable for producing them.

How to close the loop

A simple tracking mechanism makes the difference between accountability that holds and accountability that dissolves. After each monitoring discussion, record three things in the board minutes:

  • The result relative to target (on track / off track / at risk)
  • The specific commitment made in response (whether confirmed plan, changed plan, or accountability conversation)
  • The date by which follow-up will occur

At the start of the next relevant meeting, the board chair or superintendent briefly reviews open commitments from prior monitoring discussions before moving to new business. This takes five minutes and transforms monitoring from episodic to continuous. When everyone in the room knows that prior commitments will be revisited, the quality of both the commitments and the follow-through improves substantially.
