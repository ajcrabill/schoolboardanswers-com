--- layout: qa title: "How do we know if our board is spending the right amount of time on student outcomes?" category: "Monitor Progress" ---

The most reliable measure is a time-use analysis of your actual meeting agendas and minutes. Here's how to do it:

Step 1: Gather your last 6 months of agendas.

You need a sample large enough to see the pattern.

Step 2: Categorize each agenda item.

Every item falls into one of three buckets:

  • Outcomes: direct student outcome monitoring, meaning reviewing data on whether students are learning specific things
  • Operations: staffing, facilities, budget line items, vendor contracts, program approvals
  • Governance administration: board policies, board training, board elections, public comment

Step 3: Estimate time per item.

You don't need to be precise. Approximate is fine.

Step 4: Calculate the percentage.

What share of total board meeting time was spent in the Outcomes category?

What you'll find

When you run this analysis, most boards discover that Outcomes represents 5–15% of their meeting time. Operations usually consumes 60–80%.

What the target is

The target is at least 50% of meeting time on monitoring student outcome progress. This doesn't mean ignoring operations. It means your board has structured itself so that operational approval items are handled efficiently — consent agendas are your friend here — and the meat of each meeting is actual engagement with student outcome data.

Time use is a symptom, not a cause

Boards spend time on operations because they've taken on a management role, approving operational decisions that the superintendent should be making within board-established parameters. A time-use fix that doesn't address the underlying governance-management boundary won't hold. The goal is a board that governs so clearly that operations run well without the board in the middle of them.

Practical steps

  1. Run the time-use analysis before your next board meeting — pull the last six months of agendas, categorize each item, and estimate time. Share the numbers with your board without editorializing. Let the data land first.
  2. Ask one question before proposing changes: "Is this how we intended to spend our time?" The gap between intention and practice is the conversation that needs to happen before any agenda restructure.
  3. Move routine approvals to a consent agenda if your board hasn't already. If your board currently spends 90 minutes per meeting on reports, approvals, and updates and 20 minutes on student outcome discussion, inverting that ratio is the single highest-leverage governance change most boards can make.
  4. Redesign your meeting template so outcome monitoring appears first — not last — on the agenda. Items at the end get cut when meetings run long. Your goals shouldn't be what gets cut.
  5. Recheck the percentage in six months. Time-use drift is common. A one-time restructure rarely holds without a standing commitment to track it.
