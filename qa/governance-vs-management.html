--- layout: qa title: "What's the difference between governing and managing — and why does it matter?" category: "Focus Mindset" ---

Governing is setting direction and holding the organization accountable for results. Managing is deciding how to get there day-to-day. When boards blur this line, they undermine the superintendent's authority and distract themselves from the only question that matters: are students learning? The distinction isn't bureaucratic formality — it's the structural foundation that makes effective school governance possible.

The clearest way to draw the line

A useful test: if the decision requires knowledge of what happened in a school building yesterday, it's probably a management decision. If the decision shapes what the district is trying to accomplish and whether it's succeeding, it's a governance decision. The board owns the destination and the accountability for getting there. The superintendent owns the route.

Governing decisions include: adopting student outcome goals, approving the budget, setting policy, hiring and evaluating the superintendent, and monitoring results against goals. Managing decisions include: which curriculum to purchase, how to schedule professional development, which staff members to assign to which schools, and how to respond to a parent complaint. The board should be deeply interested in the outcomes those management decisions produce — but not in making the decisions themselves.

Why the blur happens

Board members cross into management for understandable reasons. They're elected because they care. They have backgrounds in education, business, or community organizing that make them feel qualified to weigh in on operational choices. And the boundary isn't always obvious — especially when management decisions seem to be producing bad results.

But feeling qualified to make a decision and being the right person to make it are different things. A board member might know a great deal about reading instruction and still be wrong to override the curriculum director's recommendation unilaterally. The governance structure exists precisely because distributed, coherent organizational authority produces better results than ad hoc intervention — even well-intentioned ad hoc intervention.

What micromanagement actually costs

When boards drift into management, several things happen — all of them bad:

  • The superintendent's authority erodes. Staff quickly learn that decisions made by the superintendent can be relitigated by going to a board member. This creates parallel power channels that breed confusion, resentment, and slow execution.
  • The board's attention fragments. Every hour spent on operational details is an hour not spent on goal-setting, monitoring outcomes, or superintendent accountability. The board's most important work gets crowded out by its least important work.
  • Accountability becomes impossible. If the board is involved in operational decisions, it can no longer hold the superintendent cleanly accountable for results. "We told you to do it that way" is not a platform for honest evaluation.
  • Good superintendents leave. Talented leaders do not stay in organizations where their professional authority is routinely second-guessed by a committee. Boards that micromanage tend to retain only superintendents who tolerate it — which is not a selection criterion for excellence.

The governance question that clarifies everything

When a board member is unsure whether to weigh in on something, one question cuts through most of the ambiguity: "Am I asking about the outcome, or am I asking about the method?" Asking about outcomes is governance. Asking about methods is management. A board member who wants to know whether third-grade reading scores are improving is governing. A board member who wants to know which phonics program the reading coaches are using in second period is managing — unless the board has adopted a policy that governs curriculum selection, in which case monitoring compliance with that policy is governance.

The distinction takes practice, and even experienced boards get it wrong sometimes. What separates high-functioning boards from low-functioning ones isn't perfection — it's the willingness to recognize the difference, correct course, and keep their eyes on whether students are learning.
