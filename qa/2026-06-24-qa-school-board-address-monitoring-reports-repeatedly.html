Monitor Progress

How should a school board address monitoring reports that repeatedly flag the same unresolved issues?

When monitoring reports flag the same issue repeatedly, your board must move from receiving information to demanding a clear plan. The pattern itself is the governance signal. It tells you that either the goal is unrealistic, the resources are missing, or the superintendent's plan is not working. Your role is not to solve the problem, but to ensure someone is actively solving it. You approve the goals, you hold the line on expectations, and you expect honest progress reporting.

The core principle here is that monitoring exists to drive accountability, not just awareness. Many boards treat monitoring reports as updates to receive and file. That is a mistake. When an issue appears once, it may be an anomaly. When it appears three times in a row, it is a pattern. Your board's job at that point is to ask the hard question: Is this goal achievable given our current plan and resources? If the answer is no, then the goal or the plan must change. If the answer is yes, then the superintendent needs to explain what different actions will produce different results.

A common mistake is assuming that repeated flags mean the superintendent is failing. Sometimes the goal itself was poorly set. Sometimes circumstances changed. The right approach is to treat the pattern as data, not judgment. Your board should ask: Is this goal still the right goal? If yes, what is the superintendent's revised timeline and strategy? If no, then your board should revise the goal rather than continue to receive failing reports. Both paths are legitimate governance. Continuing to receive the same bad news without changing the goal or the plan is not governance — it is just noise.

In practice, this means your board's meeting agenda should include a specific question when the same issue appears in consecutive monitoring reports. Do not just note it and move on. At the meeting, the board president should ask directly: "We see this issue flagged again. What is different now, and what will we see in the next report that shows progress?" This frames the conversation around accountability and future action rather than past failure. It also creates a clear record for the next meeting.

One complication is that board members may not agree on whether the goal or the execution is the problem. Some members may want to give the superintendent more time. Others may want to change the goal. This is healthy disagreement, but it needs to be resolved through a vote, not avoided. Your board should have a process for deciding when to adjust goals versus when to demand a new plan. Without that process, the board will keep getting the same reports without any resolution.

Here are the steps to make this work:

  1. At your next board meeting, when the monitoring report shows the same issue, explicitly ask: "What is different now, and what will we see in the next report that shows progress?"
  1. If the issue appears for the third consecutive time, add a discussion item to the agenda to decide whether the goal needs revision or the superintendent needs a revised plan.
  1. Before that discussion, review the original goal and ask the superintendent to bring a brief summary of what has been tried and what will change.
  1. If the board decides to keep the goal, establish a clear timeline for improvement and a specific definition of what success looks like in the next report.
  1. If the board decides to revise the goal, document the revision in writing and ensure the new goal is measurable and achievable.

This process does not guarantee tidy resolution. Some issues take longer than expected. Some goals prove harder than anticipated. But your board will move from passive reception to active governance, and that is the shift that matters.
