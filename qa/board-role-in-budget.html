How involved should the board be in the budget process?

← Back to all questions

The board sets direction and approves the budget. It does not build it. That distinction is not a technicality — it defines the difference between governance and management, and boards that blur it create problems for themselves, for the superintendent, and ultimately for students.

The governance question the board must ask is: does this budget advance our goals and respect our guardrails? That's a different question than the management questions staff must answer: how do we staff the elementary schools, which vendors do we use, what's the right ratio of coaches to teachers? Boards that wade into the second set of questions have abandoned the first — and left no one asking it.

What board involvement looks like at each stage

The board's role shifts as the budget cycle moves forward. At each stage, the work is different:

  1. Before drafting begins (fall): The board reaffirms its student outcome goals and articulates any resource guardrails — equity commitments, spending floors for particular programs, priorities for discretionary investment. This is the brief the superintendent takes into the building process. Without it, the superintendent is guessing at board direction.
  2. During development (winter): The board stays out of the building process. The superintendent and staff make the thousands of decisions required to construct a budget. Board members who contact staff directly during this phase — to advocate for programs, ask for favors, or express preferences — are undermining the superintendent's authority and injecting politics into a technical process.
  3. At proposal (spring): The board receives the proposed budget and evaluates it against the fall direction. This is when questions get asked, concerns get raised, and the board determines whether what was built reflects what was asked for. This is a review posture, not a co-authorship posture.
  4. At adoption: The board votes. If the budget meets the standard, approve it. If it doesn't, the board sends it back with specific written guidance — not redlined spreadsheets, but clear statements of what outcomes or guardrails the proposal fails to meet.

The governance question to keep asking

In every budget conversation, board members should be asking one version of the same question: how does this advance our goals? When the superintendent presents a new initiative, that's the question. When a department requests an increase, that's the question. When a program is proposed for reduction, that's still the question — what goal is served by this reduction, and what risk does it create?

"Show me how this moves our reading goal" is a legitimate board question. "Why are we using this vendor instead of that one?" is not. The first is governance. The second is management, and board members who ask it are spending political capital on a decision that isn't theirs to make.

When you can't trace spending to goals

If the superintendent presents a budget and you genuinely cannot connect significant portions of it to your adopted goals, that's important information. It means either the goals aren't specific enough to guide spending, or the budget wasn't built with goals as the organizing frame, or both.

The right response is not to reject the budget outright. It's to ask the superintendent to walk through the goal-to-spending logic explicitly, and to note where that logic is thin or absent. Then use the budget adoption as an opportunity to set an expectation: next year's proposal will include explicit goal-alignment documentation as part of the standard presentation package.

Why line-item scrutiny backfires

Boards that scrutinize individual line items — debating travel budgets, questioning supply costs, parsing salary schedules — often believe they are being fiscally responsible. In practice, they are doing something quite different: they are substituting their judgment for the superintendent's on operational decisions, and they are consuming time and political energy that should go toward strategic questions.

Line-item scrutiny also creates perverse incentives. When staff learn that the board will question specific expenditures, they pad budgets to absorb those cuts. The result is a ritual in which everyone performs fiscal discipline without actually achieving it. Strategic budget governance — focused on goals, outcomes, and alignment — produces better results with less friction.
