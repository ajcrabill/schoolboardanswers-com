Clarify Priorities

"How often should the board revisit or revise its student outcome goals?"

← Back to all questions

Review annually, revise strategically. An annual check asks three things: are the goals still the right goals, are the metrics still meaningful, and has the community's context changed enough to warrant a shift? Most years, the answer to all three is no — and that's fine. Consistency is a feature, not a flaw. But the annual check keeps the goals from becoming stale fixtures that no one feels authorized to question.

Review and revision are not the same thing

Boards that conflate reviewing goals with revising them create instability. If every annual check becomes an opportunity to rewrite priorities, the superintendent can't build a coherent multi-year improvement strategy — and staff learn to wait out board cycles rather than commit to implementation. The discipline is to separate the two actions: review happens every year, revision happens only when there's a genuine reason.

Legitimate reasons to revise a goal include:

  • A goal has been achieved — it's time to retire it or raise the target
  • The metric used to track the goal has been shown to be a poor proxy for the underlying outcome
  • A significant demographic or economic shift in the community has changed what students most need
  • State or federal accountability frameworks have changed in ways that make a goal redundant or misaligned
  • New board members were elected on a platform that reflects a genuine community mandate to shift priorities

What is not a legitimate reason: a board member's personal interest, a vendor pitch for a new program, or pressure from a vocal advocacy group that doesn't represent broad community values.

The annual review conversation

Structure the annual review as a dedicated work session, not a bolt-on to a regular meeting. The superintendent should prepare a brief report on each goal that addresses: current performance relative to target, trend over the past three years, whether the measurement approach is still sound, and any external factors that have materially changed the landscape. The board's job in that session is to ask hard questions — not to congratulate or commiserate, but to assess whether the strategic direction still holds.

Three questions worth asking explicitly for each goal:

  1. Is the target still ambitious but achievable? Targets that were set years ago may have become trivially easy or unrealistically distant given what's actually happened.
  2. Are we measuring what we actually care about? Graduation rate, for instance, measures completion — but not whether graduates are prepared for what comes next. Is the metric capturing the outcome or just a credential?
  3. Are there equity gaps within the overall number? A goal where the district average looks healthy but particular student groups are still falling behind is not a goal being met.

Protecting goals from short-term noise

One-year dips in outcome data almost never warrant revising a goal — they usually reflect implementation lag, assessment changes, or external disruptions like a pandemic or high staff turnover. Goals are set on multi-year horizons precisely because student learning doesn't respond to quarterly adjustments. A board that revises its goals every time a data point moves in the wrong direction signals to the organization that the goals aren't real — they're political cover that gets adjusted when they become inconvenient.

If the board is feeling pressure to revise a goal mid-cycle, pause and ask whether the real issue is the goal or the monitoring. Often, what looks like a goal problem is actually a monitoring problem: the board isn't getting enough data, frequently enough, in a useful enough format to feel confident in what they're seeing. Better monitoring almost always solves this before goal revision is warranted.

After an election: handling new priorities responsibly

New board members bring new mandates, and that's how democratic governance is supposed to work. But a new majority doesn't obligate immediate goal revision. The responsible path is to run the standard annual review with the new board in place, let new members engage with the full data picture, and make any revisions through a structured process — ideally with community input — rather than through a first-meeting resolution. Goals revised too fast after an election tend to lack the staff buy-in and community grounding they need to drive real change.
