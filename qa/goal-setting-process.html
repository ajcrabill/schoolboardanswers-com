Clarify Priorities

"What should the board's goal-setting process actually look like?"

← Back to all questions

Start with current student outcome data — not where you want to be, but where you actually are. Layer in community input on what families most need students to know and be able to do. Then draft, pressure-test, and adopt goals that are specific enough to be monitored and honest enough to reflect the gap between current reality and the community's aspirations. That's the whole process. Everything else is either supporting those steps or getting in the way of them.

Phase one: ground the board in reality

Before any goal-setting conversation, the board needs an honest picture of where students stand. This means more than the annual state report card — it means disaggregated data by grade, subject, race, income, disability status, and English learner status. It means trend data over at least three years, not a single snapshot. And it means data on leading indicators — attendance, third-grade reading, course completion rates — not just lagging outcomes like graduation.

The superintendent should present this data in a format designed for board comprehension, not technical defensibility. The goal is for every board member to be able to answer: which students are we serving well, which are we not, and how wide are those gaps? If board members leave the data session still unclear on those three questions, the data presentation failed and the goal-setting process isn't ready to proceed.

Phase two: hear from the community — deliberately

Community input on goals is not the same as a public comment period at a board meeting. That format systematically over-represents organized advocacy groups and under-represents the families of the students with the greatest needs. Genuine community engagement for goal-setting requires deliberate outreach:

  • Focus groups or listening sessions in multiple languages and held at accessible times and locations
  • Surveys distributed through channels families actually use — schools, community organizations, social media
  • Conversations in communities that are typically disengaged from school board processes
  • Questions that are specific enough to be useful: not "what do you want for your child?" but "if you had to choose the two or three outcomes that would most change your child's future, what would they be?"

The purpose of community input is not to outsource the board's decision-making. It's to ensure the goals reflect what the community values — not just what the education establishment finds technically convenient to measure.

Phase three: draft with the superintendent, not around them

Goal drafting should be a partnership between the board and the superintendent. The board brings the value judgments — what outcomes matter most, at what level of ambition. The superintendent brings operational knowledge — what's actually measurable, what timelines are realistic given current capacity, where the data systems are reliable and where they're not. Goals drafted without superintendent input often end up measuring things the district can't track or setting targets with no connection to what's achievable.

A draft goal should specify:

  • The student population it applies to (all students? a specific grade band? students with a specific characteristic?)
  • The specific outcome being measured
  • The metric or assessment used to measure it
  • The baseline (current performance)
  • The target and the year by which it should be reached

Phase four: pressure-test before you adopt

Before the board votes to adopt its goals, subject each draft to a brief stress test. Ask:

  1. Can this be monitored? Does the district currently collect this data? On what schedule? Who is responsible for reporting it?
  2. Is the target honest? A target of 5% improvement when you're currently at 30% is either very easy or very slow depending on what's realistic. The superintendent should be able to defend the ambition level with reference to comparable districts or research on what's achievable.
  3. Does this measure the outcome or the activity? "Students will complete a financial literacy course" is an activity. "Students will demonstrate financial literacy competency at grade level" is an outcome. Only the latter belongs at the goal level.
  4. Are equity gaps visible? Will this goal's monitoring surface whether different student groups are progressing differently, or will aggregate numbers hide disparities?

Goals that don't survive this test should be revised, not adopted and hoped into shape. A goal the board can't monitor is a aspiration, not an accountability tool.
