--- layout: qa title: "What should the board's goal-setting process actually look like?" category: "Clarify Priorities" ---

Most board goal-setting processes begin in the wrong place. They start with a blank whiteboard and the question "What should our goals be?" — and then spend the next several hours generating aspirations that feel important but aren't grounded in anything. The result is goals that sound good at adoption and drift into irrelevance within a year.

A process that produces durable, governing goals starts with data, not aspirations. Here's what it should look like, from first step to ratification.

Step one: start with current data

Before any conversation about what goals to set, the board needs to see where the district actually stands. The superintendent should present a clear data picture covering:

  • Current performance on key student outcome metrics — proficiency rates, graduation rates, chronic absenteeism, postsecondary readiness — broken down by student subgroup and school
  • Trend data over the past three to five years: is the district improving, flat, or declining on each metric?
  • Comparisons to peer districts, state averages, and national benchmarks where available
  • Identified equity gaps between student subgroups, with specific numbers attached

This data session should happen before any goal language is drafted. The board's job at this stage is to understand, not to decide. Questions are appropriate; proposals are premature. A board that skips this step ends up setting goals that reflect what members already believed rather than what students actually need.

Step two: layer in community input

After the board understands where the district stands, it should hear from the community about what outcomes matter most. This is not a vote — communities don't set goals, boards do. But community input grounds goals in local values and builds the public trust that sustains commitment through hard years.

Effective community input for goal-setting looks like:

  • A structured community forum — in-person or virtual — where families, staff, and community members respond to the data they've been shown, not to abstract aspirations
  • Surveys that surface priorities from groups who can't attend meetings, especially families with the least institutional access
  • Specific questions that produce usable input: "Given what you've seen about where our students stand, what outcomes matter most to you? What would success look like for your child in five years?"

Step three: draft in a small group

A full board of seven or nine people cannot draft goals in a meeting. Writing by committee produces language that is either too vague — built to satisfy everyone — or too long because every concern gets included. The right approach: the board chair and one or two other members, working with the superintendent, draft proposed goal language between meetings and bring it to the full board for reaction.

The draft should include, for each goal: the outcome statement, the current baseline, the proposed target, and the proposed deadline. Bringing all four elements to the table prevents the common dynamic where goals get adopted without a documented starting point or without a clear success threshold.

Step four: test against clear quality criteria

Before the full board votes, apply a formal quality test to each proposed goal. The board should be able to answer yes to all of these:

  • Student-focused: Does this goal describe what students will achieve — not what the district will do or provide?
  • Measurable: Do we have baseline data today? Can we track progress over time with consistent, credible numbers?
  • Ambitious: Would current trajectory get us here without significant change? If yes, raise the target.
  • Time-bound: Is there a specific deadline — not "eventually," but a year and month?
  • Monitorable: Can we receive a meaningful progress report on this at a regular board meeting?

Any goal that fails this test goes back to drafting. It is better to adopt two strong goals than five weak ones.

The superintendent's role vs. the board's role

These roles often get reversed in practice, creating problems at both ends. The superintendent's role in goal-setting is to advise on feasibility — to tell the board what the data suggests is achievable given current capacity, and what achieving more would require in terms of investment or strategy change. The superintendent should not set the ambition level; that is the board's job.

The board's role is to set the ambition level — to decide, on behalf of the community, how much progress is enough. A superintendent who says "I think we could get reading proficiency to 72% in three years" is offering feasibility input. A board that accepts 72% without asking "Is that enough?" is abdicating its governance function. If the current rate is 61% and similar districts have reached 80%, the board might reasonably push for 78% — not because it's easy, but because students deserve more than what's comfortable.

Common mistakes in goal-setting processes

  • Starting with language instead of data. Goals written before the board understands current performance tend to be aspirational rather than grounded, and unconnected to what the district most needs to address.
  • Letting the superintendent draft all the goals. When the superintendent writes the goals, the board loses its independent accountability role. The superintendent should inform the drafting process, not own it.
  • Skipping the baseline. A goal without a documented starting point can never be fairly evaluated. "We'll hit 80%" means nothing if nobody wrote down that you started at 63%.
  • Adopting goals without a monitoring plan. "How will we report progress on this, and how often?" should be answered before ratification — not six months later when the first progress report is due.
  • Confusing activity for outcome. Goals about what the district will do or implement are not student outcome goals. They belong in the superintendent's work plan, and treating them as board-level goals muddles who is accountable for what.

Timeline from data to adoption

  1. Month 1: Data presentation to the full board — current performance, three-to-five year trends, peer comparisons, subgroup gaps. Board asks questions; no goal drafting yet.
  2. Month 2: Community input — forums, surveys, structured listening. Results summarized for the board with direct quotes and themes, not just statistics.
  3. Month 3: Small group drafting — board chair, one or two members, and superintendent develop proposed goal language with baselines, targets, and deadlines.
  4. Month 4: Board workshop — full board reacts to the draft, applies quality criteria, revises language, and confirms monitoring approach.
  5. Month 5: First reading at a regular board meeting — community comment invited, final questions addressed.
  6. Month 6: Ratification — goals adopted by board vote and published publicly with baselines documented alongside each target.

Six months is not excessive — it reflects the seriousness of what the board is committing to. Goals adopted through a credible process carry real organizational weight. Goals adopted at a single workshop after two hours of whiteboard work don't. Everyone in the district can tell the difference, and they govern themselves accordingly.
