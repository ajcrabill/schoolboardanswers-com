Communicate Results

"Our board reports are too technical for parents to understand. What should we change?"

← Back to all questions

Start by swapping jargon for plain questions. Instead of "proficiency rate on state-administered summative assessments," say "how many students are reading on grade level?" That single reframe — replacing a measurement descriptor with the human question the measurement is trying to answer — changes who can engage with the report. Jargon isn't just a style problem; it signals to parents that the information isn't meant for them. Plain language signals the opposite.

The root cause: reports built for the wrong audience

Most board reports are written by administrators who live in the technical vocabulary of education — and who are primarily writing for the board and compliance reviewers, not for families. The result is documents that accurately convey data but communicate nothing to the people most affected by it. A parent who can't decode "disaggregated subgroup performance on ELA benchmark assessments" cannot be a meaningful partner in their child's education. That's a governance failure, not just a communications nuisance.

The fix requires a deliberate decision about audience. Before any report is drafted, ask: who is this for, and what do we need them to understand? If the answer includes parents and community members — and it should — the report needs to be written to that audience first, with technical appendices available for those who want the underlying methodology.

Concrete language changes to make

Work through your most common report phrases and translate each one. A few direct substitutions:

  • "Proficiency rate on state-administered summative assessments" → "share of students meeting grade-level standards on state tests"
  • "Chronic absenteeism rate" → "share of students missing 10% or more of school days"
  • "Subgroup disaggregation" → "results broken down by student group"
  • "ELA performance" → "reading and writing results"
  • "Cohort graduation rate" → "share of students who entered 9th grade and graduated on time"
  • "MTSS fidelity" → "whether intervention programs are being used as designed"

You don't have to eliminate all technical terms — some parents want the precise language. But lead with plain English and offer the technical equivalent parenthetically or in a footnote.

Structure reports around questions, not metrics

Instead of organizing a report by data source (state test results, then attendance, then graduation), organize it by question: Are students learning to read? Are students showing up? Are students graduating? Each question gets a brief answer — a single headline number or trend — followed by what it means and what the board is doing about it. This structure mirrors how a parent actually thinks about their child's school, and it forces the report to make a clear claim rather than simply presenting numbers.

Add a one-page plain-language summary

Even well-written full reports can run long. Every substantive outcome report should open with a one-page summary written for a general audience. That summary should answer three questions:

  1. What are the most important things we learned about student outcomes this period?
  2. Where are we making progress, and where are we falling short?
  3. What is the board doing in response?

The summary is not a highlights reel. It should include both good news and bad news. A summary that only features wins will be recognized as spin — and will undermine trust in the full report.

Test your reports before publishing them

Before finalizing any major report, share a draft with two or three parents who are not involved in school governance and ask them to read it and tell you what they learned. If they can accurately summarize the key findings, the report is working. If they're confused or say it "sounds like a lot of numbers," revise before publishing. This is a low-cost test that catches problems staff are too close to see.
