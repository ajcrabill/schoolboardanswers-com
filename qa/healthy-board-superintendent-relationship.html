--- layout: qa title: "What does a healthy board-superintendent relationship actually look like?" category: "Focus Mindset" ---

A healthy board-superintendent relationship is characterized by clear domain ownership, mutual candor, and a shared focus on student outcomes rather than institutional harmony. It is not conflict-free — healthy relationships include direct disagreement. What they don't include is confusion about who owns what, or a dynamic where either party hides information from the other.

Clear domain ownership

The foundation of the relationship is a well-understood division of authority. The board owns the "what": the outcome goals the district is pursuing, the policies that constrain and guide operations, and the accountability structure for measuring results. The superintendent owns the "how": the operational decisions, staffing choices, program implementations, and day-to-day management that translate board direction into results.

In a healthy relationship, both parties can articulate this division clearly — and both enforce it on themselves. The board doesn't second-guess operational decisions when outcomes are on track. The superintendent doesn't make unilateral decisions on matters of policy or strategic direction. Each party trusts the other to do their job.

What trust looks like in practice

Trust in this relationship is specific, not general. It isn't "we get along well" or "I believe the superintendent is a good person." It's demonstrated through consistent patterns:

  • The superintendent brings bad news proactively rather than waiting for the board to discover it
  • The board gives the superintendent room to manage without requiring approval for every decision
  • Both parties are honest in the evaluation process rather than treating it as a formality
  • Disagreements happen in direct conversation, not through back channels or public positioning

When trust is present, the superintendent doesn't spend energy protecting themselves from the board — they spend it on students. That's the practical value of a well-functioning relationship.

Red flags on the board side

Boards damage the relationship when they:

  • Intervene in operational decisions, especially personnel, without the superintendent's involvement
  • Make commitments to community members or staff that undercut the superintendent's authority
  • Spring surprises — new priorities, new concerns, new demands — without working through the superintendent first
  • Use the evaluation process to relitigate disagreements rather than to honestly assess results
  • Treat the superintendent's transparency about problems as evidence of incompetence rather than integrity

Red flags on the superintendent side

Superintendents damage the relationship when they:

  • Present data selectively — showing the board only the numbers that support a positive narrative
  • Delay or obscure reporting on goals that aren't being met
  • Manage around the board by building relationships with individual board members to create allies rather than informing the full board
  • Treat board questions as challenges to authority rather than as legitimate governance inquiries
  • Allow the relationship to become so collegial that honest evaluation becomes impossible

What breaks this dynamic and how to repair it

Most breakdowns trace back to a moment when one party crossed the other's domain and the other party didn't address it directly. The incursion became a pattern; the pattern became the norm; resentment accumulated. Repair requires naming the problem explicitly and agreeing — usually in a structured session with an external facilitator — on what the healthy version looks like and how each party will behave going forward. That conversation is uncomfortable. It is also far less costly than continuing with a broken dynamic or replacing a superintendent unnecessarily.
