Communicate Results

"Should the board communicate outcome results at board meetings, or through other channels?"

← Back to all questions

Both — but for different audiences and different purposes. Board meetings and external channels serve distinct functions in the board's accountability to the community. Relying on just one leaves a significant part of your audience unreached.

What board meetings are for

Board meetings are where the formal record is made. When the board reviews student outcome data, deliberates about whether goals are being met, and makes decisions in response, that happens on the public record. Community members who attend — or read the minutes or watch recordings later — can see the board holding itself and the administration accountable in real time.

This matters for governance integrity. The board's monitoring of student outcomes shouldn't happen in staff briefings or committee emails. It should happen at the board table, where the community can observe. Board meetings are the formal venue for that accountability.

Who actually attends board meetings

Most community members don't. A typical board meeting draws staff, a handful of advocates with specific agenda items, and occasionally a reporter. The parents whose children are most affected by the goals you're tracking are almost never in that room. If your only communication channel for student outcome results is agenda items at regular meetings, you're technically transparent but functionally invisible to most families.

What external channels are for

External communication reaches the people who don't come to meetings — which is most of your community. The goal isn't to replicate the board meeting in a newsletter. It's to give families a clear, plain-language answer to the question they actually have: are students in this district learning what they need to learn?

Effective external channels for communicating student outcome results include:

  • Annual community report: A brief, readable document (under four pages) showing each student outcome goal, the current result, and whether you're on track. Distributed through every school and posted prominently on the district website.
  • School newsletter summaries: A paragraph per goal, in plain language, pushed through whatever channel parents actually read — email, app notifications, printed flyers.
  • Brief social media updates: Especially useful for milestone results (end of year, mid-year checkpoints) — short, honest, with a link to the full report.
  • Town halls or community forums: For significant results — especially disappointing ones — an in-person or virtual session where families can ask questions is far more effective than a document alone.

The public comment period trap

Some boards treat the public comment period at board meetings as their primary community communication channel. This fails in two ways: it reaches almost no one, and it reverses the direction of accountability. Public comment is an input mechanism — it gives community members a chance to speak to the board. Student outcome communication is an output mechanism — it gives the board a way to speak to the community about results. They serve different purposes. Boards that rely only on the first have effectively opted out of the second.

Building a two-track system

The simplest approach: for every significant student outcome report that comes to the board, build a parallel communication plan. Before the board meeting, staff prepares a one-page plain-language summary of the results. After the board meeting — once the board has deliberated — that summary goes out through external channels. The board meeting is the governance record. The external communication is the community accountability. Both are necessary. Neither replaces the other.
