Communicate Results

"How transparent should we be about goals we're not meeting?"

← Back to all questions

Fully transparent. Boards that hide disappointing results lose the community's trust twice — once when the results come out anyway, and again when people learn the board knew and said nothing. There is no version of concealing missed goals that ends well. The question isn't whether to disclose; it's how to disclose in a way that is honest, contextual, and paired with a credible response.

Why the instinct to minimize is so costly

Boards often soften bad news out of genuine concern — they don't want to alarm families, undermine staff morale, or invite political attacks. These concerns are understandable but the logic is backward. Families already know when schools aren't working; they see it in their children every day. When the board's public communications don't match what families are experiencing, it signals that the board is either out of touch or being evasive. Either interpretation damages credibility far more than the underlying results.

Trust is built over time through consistency between what the board says and what the community observes. A board that reports honestly when things are hard earns the credibility to be believed when things improve. A board that only reports good news trains the community to discount everything it says.

What full transparency actually looks like

Transparency about missed goals means providing the actual numbers, naming the gap between where you are and where you committed to be, and explaining what you understand about why. It does not mean catastrophizing, assigning blame publicly, or speculating beyond what the data supports. A clear, factual statement of a missed goal — "We committed to 75% of third graders reading on grade level by spring. We reached 61%. That is not good enough, and here is what we know about why." — is more credible and more reassuring than vague language that tries to soften the same fact.

The right structure for disclosing a missed goal

When reporting on goals that weren't met, follow a consistent structure:

  1. State the goal and the result directly. Don't bury the shortfall inside paragraphs of context. Lead with it.
  2. Explain what you know about why. Distinguish between what the data shows and what is still being analyzed. Don't speculate, but don't hide what you do know.
  3. Describe what the board is doing in response. This is where accountability becomes visible. A missed goal without a response is a problem. A missed goal with a specific, named response is a board doing its job.
  4. Be honest about the timeline for improvement. If you don't know when results will improve, say so. Promising unrealistic timelines to soften bad news creates a third trust-loss event down the road.

What to do when you don't yet know why

Sometimes results come in and the board genuinely doesn't have a clear explanation yet. That's a legitimate situation — and it's fine to say so. "We are still analyzing what drove this result and will report back to the community by [date]" is transparent. What's not acceptable is using "we're still analyzing" as a permanent deflection. Set a specific date to return with findings and keep it.

The long-term payoff

Boards that establish a pattern of honest reporting — including about missed goals — build the kind of community credibility that becomes valuable when hard decisions need public support. When a board that has always leveled with the community proposes a budget change or asks for a bond measure, people believe it. Boards that have been selective with the truth rarely get that benefit of the doubt, even when they've earned it.
