--- layout: qa title: "Our superintendent's reports are full of numbers but never tell us whether students are actually improving. What do we ask for?" category: "Monitor Progress" ---

Ask for three specific things per goal — nothing more, nothing less. Until the board defines exactly what it needs to see, staff will default to comprehensive. Comprehensive means twenty pages of data and no clear answer to the only question that matters: are students better off than they were?

The board's job is not to evaluate data. It is to evaluate whether the superintendent's plan is producing the outcomes the board set as goals. That requires a completely different kind of report than what most districts produce.

The three questions to ask for, per goal

For each student outcome goal the board has adopted, request that the monitoring report answer exactly these three questions:

  1. What is the target? The specific, measurable result the board set — not a program name, not a strategic priority, not a direction. A number, a date, and a metric. "Increase the percentage of students reading at grade level by third grade from 51% to 62% by June 2026."
  2. What are the current results? The most recent actual data, stated plainly alongside the target and the prior year. One line, not a slide deck. "Current: 57%. Target: 62%. Prior year: 51%."
  3. Is the district on track, and why? A direct judgment from the superintendent — on track, off track, or at risk — with a brief explanation. This is where most reports fall silent. Producing that judgment is the point. It forces the person running the district to go on record about whether the plan is working.

If staff cannot produce these three answers, that is itself important information — either the goal isn't measurable enough to monitor, or the district lacks the data infrastructure to track it. Both are problems the board needs to know about.

The difference between compliance reporting and outcome reporting

Compliance reporting tells the board what the district did — programs launched, trainings completed, policies adopted, funds spent. Outcome reporting tells the board what changed for students as a result. Most districts default to compliance reporting because it is easier to produce and almost always looks good. The district did the things it said it would do. Whether those things improved student learning is a different — and harder — question.

The shift the board needs to make is from accepting activity data as evidence of progress to requiring outcome data as the only evidence that counts. "We trained 150 teachers in the new reading curriculum" is not a monitoring report. "The percentage of students meeting reading benchmarks increased from 51% to 57%, against a target of 62%" is.

How to request the format change constructively

Bring a template. Don't ask the superintendent to figure out what you want — show them. A one-page summary with three labeled fields per goal (Target / Current Result / On-Track Assessment) eliminates ambiguity and makes it easy for staff to comply. Frame the request as a time-efficiency move for the board, not a criticism of current reports. "We want to use our meeting time for discussion rather than orientation, and this format would help us get there faster."

Then adopt the format in board policy. A format agreed to informally gets renegotiated every time staff turns over. A format embedded in policy becomes a standing expectation — it survives superintendent transitions and doesn't require the board to re-litigate the request.

Transitioning from current practice

Don't try to convert all reports at once. Pick one goal — ideally the board's highest-priority student outcome goal — and pilot the three-question format at the next monitoring date. Let the superintendent experience what it requires to produce it. Debrief after: What was hard to produce? What data doesn't exist yet? What does the board need to understand about data availability cycles? Use that conversation to refine the format before rolling it out to all goals.

The transition from data dumps to monitoring reports usually takes one to two cycles. After that, most boards wonder how they ever governed without them.
