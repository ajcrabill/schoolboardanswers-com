Align Resources

"How do we handle board members who protect specific budget items for political reasons?"

← Back to all questions

The most effective tool is a shared, explicit standard: we fund what moves our goals. When a protected line item comes up, ask the member to explain how it advances a specific student outcome goal. That question doesn't attack the person — it applies the same test to their favored program that the board applies to every other program. If the item passes the test, it deserves to be funded and the debate is over. If it can't pass the test, the conversation has shifted from "do you support this program" to "do you support our own standards."

Why this happens — and why it's so common

Political protection of budget items is nearly universal on school boards, and it usually isn't cynical. Board members run for office with specific constituencies, relationships, and causes. A member elected with support from a particular employee group, community organization, or parent network often feels a genuine obligation to those relationships. The problem isn't bad faith — it's misaligned role definition. Board members who haven't fully internalized the shift from advocate to governance fiduciary will default to protecting "their" things when pressure comes.

This is also contagious. Once one member establishes that personal advocacy is acceptable, others adopt the same behavior to stay even. The result is a board where every member has a protected list, the superintendent can't make resource decisions without political calculation, and the budget process becomes a negotiation among board members rather than a strategic allocation process.

Prevention is more effective than confrontation

The time to address this problem is before a specific program comes under scrutiny — not during a heated budget discussion when a member is already publicly invested. Preventive moves include:

  • Establishing the standard before budget season. In the fall, before any budget drafting occurs, the board should formally reaffirm its student outcome goals and adopt an explicit policy that resource decisions are evaluated against those goals. This creates the standard in a neutral context, when no specific program is at stake.
  • Applying the standard universally and early. The first time the outcomes-alignment question is asked should not be when a politically sensitive item is on the table. If the board regularly asks "how does this connect to our goals?" about popular, uncontroversial programs, the question becomes routine rather than adversarial.
  • Board self-evaluation. Annual board self-assessment that explicitly includes a question about whether members are advocating for specific programs or constituencies at the board table creates accountability that doesn't require one member to confront another.

When it's already happening

If a member is actively protecting a line item and the standard isn't yet established, the board chair has the most leverage. In a work session or budget discussion, the chair can redirect the conversation: "Before we debate this specific item, can we agree on how we'll evaluate all of the items? What's our standard?" That framing makes the standard the topic rather than the program — and it's harder to oppose a fair process than to defend a single program.

If the chair is the member doing the protecting, the problem is harder. In that case, other members need to be willing to name the standard themselves, even if it creates friction. The alternative — letting political protection become the norm — is significantly worse for students and for the board's credibility.

What you cannot do

You cannot eliminate the reality that board members have relationships, histories, and constituencies. Nor should you try — those connections are part of why community members run for the board. What you can do is ensure that those relationships don't override the board's commitment to its own stated goals. The goal is not a board of dispassionate technicians. It's a board that cares deeply about its community and holds itself to the discipline of asking whether its resource decisions actually help students learn.
