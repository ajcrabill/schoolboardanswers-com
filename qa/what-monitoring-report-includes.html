Monitor Progress

"What should a good board monitoring report include?"

← Back to all questions

Three things: the goal being monitored, current performance data compared to the target and to prior periods, and a clear interpretation of whether you're on track, off track, or at risk. A monitoring report that provides data without interpretation shifts the board's role from governance to analysis — and most boards aren't equipped to do that analysis well in a live meeting. A report that provides interpretation without data is just an assertion. You need both.

Element one: restate the goal and its target

Every monitoring report should open with a precise restatement of the goal being reviewed: the outcome, the metric, the baseline, and the adopted target with its deadline. This isn't administrative throat-clearing — it's the standard against which everything else in the report is being evaluated. Boards that skip this step often end up debating whether progress is good or bad without a shared reference point for what "good" actually means.

If the goal was formally adopted as "80% of third-grade students will read at or above grade level on the state assessment by 2027, up from 61% in 2024," the monitoring report should say exactly that before showing any data. The target is the accountability contract. Keeping it visible prevents the conversation from drifting toward whether the superintendent is trying hard enough and keeps it focused on whether students are learning.

Element two: current data with the right comparisons

A single data point tells you almost nothing. Good monitoring data shows current performance in at least three comparisons:

  • Against the target: Where do we need to be by the deadline, and where are we now? What's the remaining gap?
  • Against prior periods: Is performance moving in the right direction? How much did it move this year compared to last year?
  • Disaggregated by student group: Are the overall numbers masking wide variation? Which student populations are progressing and which are not?

The disaggregated view is not optional. A district where third-grade reading proficiency rose from 61% to 68% overall looks like progress — until you see that proficiency among low-income students stayed flat at 42% while it rose from 74% to 83% among higher-income students. The aggregate number represents the average of two very different realities. Boards that monitor only aggregates end up celebrating progress that isn't reaching the students who need it most.

Element three: a superintendent interpretation

The superintendent — not staff analysts, not consultants — should provide a written interpretation of what the data means. This interpretation should answer four questions:

  1. Are we on track to meet the goal by the target date? Not hedged or qualified — a direct yes, no, or at risk, with a brief explanation of why.
  2. What is driving current performance? What factors — instructional, demographic, operational — explain the trend?
  3. What is already being done in response? If performance is lagging, what has the superintendent directed, and when should the board expect to see those actions reflected in the data?
  4. What, if anything, does the board need to decide or know? Is there a resource gap, a policy barrier, or a strategic risk the board should be aware of?

This interpretation is where superintendent accountability actually lives. It's easy to present data; it's much harder to stand behind a written assessment of whether you're succeeding or failing and explain what you're doing about it. A monitoring system without this element lets leaders hide behind numbers rather than answer for them.

What monitoring reports should not include

Good monitoring reports are focused. Several things that commonly appear in board reports don't belong in a goal monitoring report and tend to dilute rather than sharpen accountability:

  • Program descriptions and activity lists — these describe inputs, not outcomes
  • Testimonials and anecdotes — these are useful for community communication, not governance monitoring
  • Comparisons to state or national averages that aren't referenced in the adopted goal — these shift the standard mid-stream
  • Data on goals not currently being monitored — monitoring should be focused; include only the goal under review

Frequency and format

Each goal should be formally monitored at least once per year, with a brief check-in at mid-year for any goal rated "at risk." The format should be standardized across all goals so the board develops fluency with the structure — a consistent one- or two-page report per goal, not a custom presentation built from scratch each time. Standardization reduces the cognitive load on board members and makes it harder to obscure weak results with impressive-looking visuals.

The board's role in a monitoring session is to read the report before the meeting, ask clarifying questions, and make a formal determination: does the superintendent's interpretation and response adequately address what the data shows? If yes, acknowledge it and move on. If no, direct a specific follow-up. That's the full scope of the board's job in a monitoring session — not to solve the problem, but to determine whether the person responsible for solving it has a credible plan.
