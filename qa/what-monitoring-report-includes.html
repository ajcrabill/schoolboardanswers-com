--- layout: qa title: "What should a good board monitoring report include?" category: "Monitor Progress" ---

A monitoring report is not a presentation. It is not a dashboard, an annual report, or a general update. It is a structured answer to one question: relative to the goal the board set, where do results stand right now? A good monitoring report is readable in five minutes and leaves no ambiguity about whether the district is on track.

Most boards receive reports that don't meet this standard — not because staff are withholding information, but because no one has defined what a monitoring report is supposed to do. The result is comprehensive documents that require interpretation rather than summaries that enable decisions.

The three required elements

Every monitoring report — regardless of format, length, or goal type — must include exactly three things:

  1. The goal: The specific, measurable outcome the board adopted, stated plainly. Not a program description or strategic priority — the actual target. Example: "Increase the percentage of 8th graders scoring proficient or above in math from 42% to 55% by June 2026."
  2. Current data compared to target and prior periods: The most recent result, shown against both the goal and the prior year or prior period. This comparison is what makes the number meaningful. A result of 48% is good news or bad news depending on whether the goal is 45% or 55%, and whether the trend is up from 39% or down from 52%.
  3. An on-track interpretation: A direct judgment from the superintendent — on track, off track, or at risk — with a brief explanation. This is the piece most reports omit. Without it, the board is left to draw its own conclusions from raw data, which produces inconsistent readings and unfocused discussion.

What to exclude

The most common mistake in monitoring reports is including material that belongs somewhere else. The one-page summary should not contain:

  • Descriptions of programs or initiatives underway — those belong in an appendix or a separate operational update
  • Input and process data presented as outcome evidence ("we trained 80 teachers" is not evidence students are learning more)
  • Comparisons to state averages or peer districts, unless the board's goal is explicitly benchmarked to those comparisons
  • Explanations of why a gap exists — context is useful but should follow the status judgment, not replace it

Every element that doesn't answer "are we on track?" adds noise. Noise makes discussion harder and gives off-track results more places to hide.

Format: digestible in five minutes

A monitoring report summary should fit on one page per goal. Board members read it before the meeting. The meeting discussion begins from the superintendent's on-track judgment — not from the raw data itself. Supporting data, trend charts, and subgroup breakdowns go in appendices for members who want to go deeper, but the appendix is background, not the starting point.

If a monitoring report requires a verbal presentation to explain what it says, it hasn't done its job. The report should communicate independently. The superintendent's role at the meeting is to field questions and discuss implications — not to translate a document the board couldn't read on its own.

The difference between a monitoring report and a presentation

A presentation is something staff prepares to communicate what they want the board to know. A monitoring report is something built around what the board needs to know: whether the goal it set is being achieved. The distinction changes who controls the agenda. In a presentation, staff decides what to emphasize. In a monitoring report structured around a board-adopted goal, the goal controls what gets reported. Staff answers the board's question — not the question staff would prefer to answer.

How to ask staff to restructure reports

Frame the request as a format change, not a critique of current work. Tell the superintendent: "We want a one-page summary for each adopted goal showing the target, current results, and your on-track assessment. Supporting detail can go in an appendix." Bring a template. Offer to pilot it with one goal before rolling it out to all goals. Then adopt the format in board policy — so it survives staff transitions and doesn't have to be renegotiated each year.
